# Training Plan Builder Skill
> For recreational second-marathon runners

---

## Sources Synthesized

- **Daniels' Running Formula** (Jack Daniels) — pace zones, VDOT, workout structure
- **Hansons Marathon Method** (Luke & Kevin Hanson) — cumulative fatigue, no long run over 16 miles
- **80/20 Running** (Matt Fitzgerald) — intensity distribution, polarized training
- **Advanced Marathoning** (Pfitzinger & Douglas) — medium-long runs, periodization
- **Run Less Run Faster / FIRST** (Furman Institute) — three quality runs, high intensity

## 1. Plan Architecture — Phase Structure

A 16–20 week plan divides into four phases. The longer the plan, the more time in base and build.

### Phase Breakdown

| Phase | 16-Week Plan | 18-Week Plan | 20-Week Plan | Purpose |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Base | Weeks 1–3 | Weeks 1–4 | Weeks 1–5 | Aerobic foundation, consistent easy volume, strides |
| Build | Weeks 4–9 | Weeks 5–11 | Weeks 6–13 | Introduce quality, progressive long runs, MP work |
| Peak | Weeks 10–13 | Weeks 12–15 | Weeks 14–17 | Highest volume and intensity, race-specific work |
| Taper | Weeks 14–16 | Weeks 16–18 | Weeks 18–20 | Volume reduction, intensity maintained, freshening |

### Phase Characteristics

**Base Phase**
- All runs at easy/conversational effort (Zone 1–2 in 80/20 terms)
- Introduce strides (4–6 x 20 seconds at mile effort) 2x/week at end of easy runs
- No structured workouts; build the habit of running by feel
- Weekly mileage increases no more than 10% per week
- Source: Pfitzinger emphasizes base aerobic development before any quality work

**Build Phase**
- Introduce one quality session per week (tempo or cruise intervals) in weeks 1–2 of build
- Add second quality session (intervals or MP run) in weeks 3–4 of build
- Long run begins incorporating marathon pace segments in the second half of build
- Recovery week every 3–4 weeks (drop volume 20–30%)
- Source: Daniels recommends introducing quality gradually; Hansons introduces MP work early

**Peak Phase**
- Highest training stress of the cycle
- Two to three quality sessions per week (depending on volume level)
- Long runs at their longest; Pfitzinger peaks at 20–22 miles, Hansons caps at 16 with cumulative fatigue logic
- Medium-long runs (13–16 miles at easy pace) added midweek at higher volume levels
- Final peak week is typically 2–3 weeks before race day

**Taper Phase**
- Volume drops 20–25% per week across 3 weeks (16-week plan) or maintains a gentler 2-week taper
- Intensity does NOT drop — keep one quality session per week at goal pace
- Long run drops to 12–14 miles two weeks out, 8–10 miles race week
- Source: Daniels and Pfitzinger both emphasize maintaining intensity while cutting volume

## 2. Weekly Mileage Progression

### Setting the Starting Volume

Start at a volume the athlete can sustain comfortably — typically 80–85% of current weekly mileage to leave room for progression. Never start higher than current fitness supports.

| Current Fitness Level | Starting Weekly Mileage |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| Running 20–25 mpw | 22–25 mpw |
| Running 25–35 mpw | 28–32 mpw |
| Running 35–45 mpw | 38–42 mpw |
| Running 45+ mpw | 45–50 mpw |

### Progression Rules

**10% Rule (Daniels / Pfitzinger):** Never increase total weekly mileage by more than 10% in a single week.

**3-weeks-up / 1-week-down cycle:** Build for 3 consecutive weeks, then take a recovery week at 70–75% of the previous peak week. Repeat.

**Peak Mileage Targets by Volume Level:**

| Volume Level | Peak Week (single highest week) | Typical Peak Phase Average |
|-------------|--------------------------------|---------------------------|
| 30 mpw plan | 35–38 miles | 32–35 miles |
| 40 mpw plan | 45–50 miles | 42–47 miles |
| 50 mpw plan | 55–60 miles | 52–57 miles |

### Taper Mileage

- Week 3 before race: peak week mileage
- Week 2 before race: 65–70% of peak
- Race week: 40–50% of peak (including the race itself)

### Recovery Week Guidelines

Recovery weeks are non-negotiable. Signs that a recovery week is overdue:
- Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above baseline for 3 consecutive days
- Persistent heavy legs after two consecutive easy days
- Motivation or mood decline
- Sleep quality degrading despite normal life stress

## 3. Quality Workout Selection

### The Three Workout Types

**Tempo Runs (Lactate Threshold Work)**
- Pace: Approximately 25–30 seconds per mile slower than 10K race pace; "comfortably hard," sustainable for ~60 minutes
- Use Daniels' VDOT table or calculate as roughly 83–88% of max heart rate
- Duration: 20–40 minutes continuous tempo, or cruise intervals (3–4 x mile at tempo with 60-second jog recovery)
- Frequency: Once per week throughout build and peak phases
- Source: Daniels considers tempo the most race-specific workout for the marathon

**Interval / VO2max Work**
- Pace: Approximately 5K to mile race pace; "hard but controlled"
- Use sparingly — Pfitzinger and 80/20 both caution against overuse at marathon distance
- Frequency: Once per week during build only; reduce in peak phase as MP work takes precedence
- Source: FIRST uses intervals as a core session; Pfitzinger uses them early in build, not peak

**Interval Format Rotation — MANDATORY**

Never repeat the same interval format two weeks in a row. Rotate across the menu below to vary stimulus, prevent mental staleness, and develop different energy system contributions.

| Format | Total Quality Volume | Best For |
|--------|---------------------|----------|
| 6 × 800m @ 5K pace, 90-sec jog recovery | ~5 km | Standard VO2max block |
| 5 × 1000m @ 5K pace, 2-min jog | ~5 km | Slightly longer sustained effort |
| 4 × 1200m @ 5K–10K pace, 2-min jog | ~5 km | Bridging toward tempo |
| 3 × 1600m @ 10K pace, 2.5-min jog | ~5 km | Race-pace endurance |
| 10 × 400m @ mile pace, 60-sec jog | ~4 km | Speed / leg turnover |
| 8 × 600m @ 5K pace, 90-sec jog | ~5 km | Medium volume, sharp pace |
| 2 × (4 × 400m) @ mile–5K, 60-sec / 3-min set rest | ~3.2 km | Structured set work |
| Fartlek: 6 × 3 min hard / 2 min easy (time-based) | varies | Less rigid, good for fatigue weeks |
| Ladder: 400–600–800–1000–800–600–400m @ 5K pace | ~4.6 km | Variety within a single session |

**Rotation rule:** Pick the next unused format from the list. After completing the full menu, start again — but shift the pace targets slightly (e.g., target 5K pace one cycle, then mile pace the next) to keep adaptation fresh.

**Volume cap:** Total interval volume (hard reps only, excluding WU/CD) should not exceed 8 km in a single session for athletes under 50 mpw.

**Marathon Pace (MP) Runs**
- Pace: Goal marathon pace — this is the most race-specific training stimulus
- Format: 10–18 miles with the middle 6–12 miles at MP; or a standalone 8–12 mile MP run
- Frequency: Once per week during peak phase; every 10–14 days during build
- Source: Hansons Marathon Method centers the entire plan on MP runs; Pfitzinger includes MP in long runs

### How Many Quality Sessions Per Week

| Volume Level | Base Phase | Build Phase | Peak Phase |
|-------------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| 30 mpw | 0 | 1 | 1–2 |
| 40 mpw | 0–1 | 2 | 2 |
| 50 mpw | 1 | 2 | 2–3 |

**Rule:** Quality sessions should never exceed 20% of weekly volume. A 30-mpw runner should not do more than 6 miles of quality work per week.

### Workout Priority Order

When forced to choose, prioritize in this order:
1. The long run (non-negotiable)
2. The tempo or MP run (race-specific)
3. The interval session (optional at marathon distance)

## 4. Long Run Planning

### Progressive Long Run Schedule

The long run is the cornerstone of marathon training. It should increase gradually and peak 2–3 weeks before race day.

**Progression Formula:**
- Start the long run at roughly 25–30% of weekly mileage
- Add 1–2 miles every 1–2 weeks
- Take a step-back long run (reduce by 2–3 miles) every 3–4 weeks, aligned with recovery weeks
- Peak long run 3 weeks before the race; reduce significantly for taper

**Peak Long Run Distance:**

| Method | Peak Long Run |
|--------|--------------|
| Pfitzinger | 20–22 miles |
| Daniels | 18–20 miles (not over 2.5 hours) |
| Hansons | 16 miles (cumulative fatigue justification) |
| FIRST | 20–22 miles |
| 80/20 | 20 miles |

**Recommendation for second-marathon runners:** Target 20 miles as the peak long run, reached 3 weeks before race day. Hansons' 16-mile cap is appropriate only if total weekly volume is high (50+ mpw) and midweek medium-long runs are included.

### Long Run Pacing

- **Base and early build:** Pure easy pace — 60–90 seconds per mile slower than goal MP, fully conversational. Do not race the long run.
- **Late build:** Last 3–4 miles at MP (optional "progression" format)
- **Peak phase:** Miles 13–16 at MP within a 20-mile run (Pfitzinger's race-simulation approach)

### When to Include MP Segments in Long Runs

| Phase | Long Run Format |
|-------|----------------|
| Base | Easy pace throughout |
| Build (early) | Easy with last 2–3 miles at MP |
| Build (late) | Easy for first half, MP for last 4–6 miles |
| Peak | Easy for first 8–10 miles, MP for 8–10 miles |
| Taper | Easy pace only; shorter distances |

**Important:** MP-segment long runs are demanding. Do not schedule another quality session within 3 days on either side.

### Long Run Recovery

- The 48 hours after a long run should be fully easy or rest
- Pfitzinger recommends a "medium-long run" (13–16 miles easy) midweek at 50+ mpw to supplement the long run
- Never schedule a quality session the day before or after the long run

## 6. Adjustment Decision Tree

Use this decision tree when athlete feedback signals a need to modify the plan.

### Decision Tree

```
Is the athlete completing all scheduled workouts?
│
├── NO — What is the cause?
│   ├── Life/schedule conflict → Prioritize: Long run > Tempo/MP > Intervals > Easy
│   ├── Fatigue/soreness → Is it muscular or systemic?
│   │   ├── Muscular (legs heavy, not sick) → Replace quality with easy; reassess next week
│   │   └── Systemic (elevated HR, mood, sleep) → Declare unplanned recovery week; drop 30% volume
│   └── Injury → See Injury Protocol below
│
└── YES — How is the athlete feeling during quality sessions?
    │
    ├── Cannot hit target paces → Is this consistent over 2+ sessions?
    │   ├── YES → Reassess VDOT / goal pace; may need to adjust target MP down 5–10 sec/mile
    │   └── NO (one bad day) → Note it; continue plan
    │
    ├── Hitting paces easily, feels like too little effort →
    │   ├── Check: Are easy runs truly easy? (80/20 audit — see Section 7)
    │   ├── If easy runs are too fast → Slow them down before adding intensity
    │   └── If distribution is correct → May reassess goal pace upward conservatively
    │
    └── On track → Continue as planned
```

### Injury Protocol

**Minor soreness (< 3/10, localized, gone after warm-up):**
- Continue running, reduce volume by 20%
- Replace harder sessions with easy running
- Monitor daily

**Moderate pain (3–6/10, persists during run, or present next morning):**
- Stop running for 2–3 days; cross-train if pain-free
- Do not attempt to "make up" missed miles — skip them entirely
- Return with easy running only; reintroduce quality after 5–7 pain-free days

**Significant pain (> 6/10, affects gait, or does not resolve with rest):**
- Stop all running
- Seek professional evaluation
- Plan restart based on medical guidance; do not attempt to preserve training block at the expense of race-day health

### Volume Modification Guidelines

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Athlete missed 1 quality session | Do not make it up; continue plan |
| Athlete missed the long run | Attempt long run the following day if schedule allows; otherwise skip |
| Athlete missed a full week | Return at 80% of missed week's volume; do not attempt to catch up |
| Athlete missed 2+ weeks | Reassess whether current race is the target; consider dropping to half marathon or later marathon |
| Athlete completed all workouts but feels flat | Insert an unplanned recovery week; this is a sign of accumulated fatigue |

## 7. Integrating 80/20 Distribution

### The Core Principle

80/20 Running (Fitzgerald, based on polarized training research) prescribes that approximately **80% of weekly training volume should be at easy/low intensity** (conversational, Zone 1–2) and **no more than 20% at moderate-to-hard intensity** (threshold, interval, or MP pace).

The most common mistake in recreational marathon training is running easy runs too fast, which pushes too much volume into the "moderate" zone and creates chronic fatigue without sufficient aerobic adaptation.

### Defining the Zones

Use one of three methods to define intensity zones:

**Heart Rate Method (most reliable):**
- Zone 1 (Easy): < 76% of max HR
- Zone 2 (Moderate-Easy): 76–85% of max HR
- Zone 3 (Threshold): 86–90% of max HR
- Zone 4 (Hard): 91–95% of max HR
- Zone 5 (Very Hard): > 95% of max HR

**80/20 Distribution:** Zones 1–2 = 80%; Zones 3–5 = 20%

**Pace Method (approximate):**
- Easy: 90+ seconds per mile slower than goal MP
- Moderate: 60–90 seconds per mile slower than goal MP (avoid spending time here)
- Threshold/MP: 0–30 seconds per mile slower than goal MP
- Interval: At or faster than 5K pace

**Talk Test:**
- Easy: Can speak in full sentences without breathing interruption
- Threshold: Can say a few words but not full sentences
- Hard: Single words only

### Weekly Distribution Audit

Run this audit at the end of each week:

1. Log all miles with their effort level (easy, moderate, threshold, hard)
2. Calculate: (Easy miles + Moderate-easy miles) / Total miles × 100
3. Target: This number should be ≥ 80%

**Example Audit — 40 mpw week:**

| Session | Miles | Zone |
|---------|-------|------|
| Easy Tuesday | 6 | Easy |
| Tempo Thursday (WU+CD) | 4 | Easy |
| Tempo Thursday (tempo portion) | 4 | Hard |
| Easy Friday | 5 | Easy |
| Long run (first 14 miles) | 14 | Easy |
| Long run (last 4 miles at MP) | 4 | Hard |
| Easy Sunday | 5 | Easy |
| **Total** | **42 miles** | |

Easy miles: 34. Hard miles: 8. Ratio: 34/42 = **81% easy.** This passes the 80/20 audit.

### Common Distribution Mistakes

**Running easy runs too fast:**
- Symptom: Easy runs feel "fine" but are at 80–82% max HR
- Fix: Slow down until heart rate is below 75–76% max HR; this will feel embarrassingly slow at first — that is correct
- Source: Fitzgerald notes that most recreational runners run easy days at moderate intensity, undermining recovery and blunting adaptation

**Miscounting MP miles as "easy":**
- Marathon pace is NOT easy effort — it is hard/threshold work
- Always count MP miles in the "hard" column for the audit

**Accumulating moderate-intensity miles:**
- The "gray zone" (60–85% max HR) is the most fatiguing intensity with the least adaptive return
- Easy runs that are "sort of easy" and tempo runs that are "sort of tempo" create this problem
- Fix: Make easy runs definitively easy; make hard runs definitively hard

### Corrective Actions When Distribution is Off

| Audit Result | Action |
|-------------|--------|
| < 75% easy | Slow all easy runs by 30–45 seconds per mile for 2 weeks; re-audit |
| 75–80% easy | Minor correction; slow easy runs by 15–20 seconds per mile |
| 80–85% easy | On target — maintain |
| > 90% easy | May be under-stimulating; check whether quality sessions are hitting target intensities |
