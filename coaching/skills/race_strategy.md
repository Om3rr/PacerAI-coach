# Race Strategy Skill

---

## Overview

This document defines the race strategy framework for marathon coaching. It is designed to be used by an AI coach when advising athletes on race execution, pre-race preparation, and post-race analysis. All guidance is evidence-based and calibrated for recreational runners targeting their second marathon. Apply judgment based on individual athlete data, training history, and race conditions.

---

## 1. Pace Planning Methodology

### Foundational Principle

Pace targets must be derived from **training data**, not from goal times set weeks or months before a training block begins. The goal time is the output of the process, not the input.

### Step-by-Step Pace Band Construction

**Step 1 — Establish the Training-Based Time Predictor**

Use the best available race predictor from the final 6 weeks of training. Priority order:

1. A recent tune-up race (half marathon, 10K) run at full effort in race conditions
2. The athlete's best long run progression run (e.g., last 10 miles at marathon goal pace)
3. VO2max-derived prediction from a fitness tracker (use with caution; treat as a ceiling, not a target)

**Step 2 — Apply the Recreational Runner Correction Factor**

Standard predictors (Riegel formula, McMillan) are calibrated for well-trained runners. Recreational runners in their second marathon should add a correction:

| Source Predictor | Correction |
|---|---|
| Half marathon time (recent race) | Multiply by 2.11 (not 2.09) |
| 10K time (recent race) | Multiply by 4.66 |
| VO2max-based tool | Add 3–5% to predicted finish time |
| Long run progression | Conservative: assume 15 sec/mile slower than long run pace |

**Step 3 — Set the Pace Band**

A pace band is a range, not a single number. Construct it as:

- **Ceiling pace (fastest):** Target time per mile for a perfect-conditions day
- **Floor pace (slowest):** Ceiling pace + 20 sec/mile — the pace that still achieves a meaningful result

| Zone | Definition | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Ceiling | Target pace for ideal conditions | Cool weather, legs feel good through mile 8 |
| Midpoint | Ceiling + 10 sec/mile | Most common scenario |
| Floor | Ceiling + 20 sec/mile | Heat, fatigue, off day — still finish strong |

**Step 4 — Adjust for Conditions**

Apply these adjustments to the ceiling pace before race morning:

| Condition | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Temperature 55–60°F (ideal) | No adjustment |
| Temperature 60–65°F | Add 10 sec/mile |
| Temperature 65–70°F | Add 20–30 sec/mile |
| Temperature 70–75°F | Add 30–45 sec/mile |
| Temperature >75°F | Treat as a training run; abandon time goal |
| Significant wind (>15 mph headwind segments) | Add 10–15 sec/mile on exposed sections |
| Course with >400 ft net elevation gain | Add 5–10 sec/mile overall |
| Rain (cold rain) | Add 5–10 sec/mile; blister and chafing risk increases |

**Step 5 — Print the Pace Band**

Write splits for every 5K, every 10K, and miles 20, 22, 24, and 26. Include both ceiling and floor numbers. The athlete wears this on their wrist or tucks it in their shorts pocket.

## 2. Split Strategy: Negative vs. Even vs. Positive

### The Evidence Summary

| Strategy | Definition | Outcome for Recreational Marathon Runners |
|---|---|---|
| Negative split | Second half faster than first half | Optimal finish time; rare to achieve spontaneously |
| Even split | Both halves within ~1 minute of each other | Strongly correlated with PR performance |
| Positive split | First half faster than second half | The most common outcome; >80% of recreational runners |

**Key finding:** Among recreational runners in their second marathon, the single most predictive variable for a strong finish is the pace differential between miles 1–5 and goal pace. Every 10 seconds per mile too fast in the opening 5 miles correlates with a 20–30 second per mile slowdown after mile 20.

### Target: Controlled Even Split with Slight Negative Bias

For a recreational runner in their second marathon, the goal is:

- First half: goal pace + 5–10 seconds/mile (slightly conservative)
- Second half: goal pace to goal pace − 5 seconds/mile (if feeling strong after mile 18)
- Miles 20–26.2: hold or push by feel — never by plan

Do not target an aggressive negative split. The goal is to arrive at mile 20 feeling like you have run 15 miles. The back half of the race takes care of itself if the first half is disciplined.

### Why Recreational Runners Go Out Too Fast

1. Taper-induced energy surge — legs feel fresh and deceivingly good
2. Crowd pace — the race environment pulls runners forward
3. First-mile adrenaline — GPS watches lag, perceived effort is misleading
4. Optimism bias — "I feel amazing, today is the day"

Counter each of these explicitly in pre-race briefings.

## 3. Mile-by-Mile Checkpoints

### Miles 1–10: The Conservative Bank

**Physical goals:**
- Run 10–15 seconds per mile slower than goal pace for miles 1–3
- Settle into goal pace by mile 4
- Heart rate should feel easy — if you cannot hold a conversation, you are too fast
- Do not look at pace in mile 1; run by feel

**Mental goals:**
- Let runners pass you; they will come back to you after mile 18
- Focus on form: cadence, arm swing, relaxed jaw and shoulders
- Treat every mile as a check-in, not a competition

**Checkpoints:**

| Mile | Check |
|---|---|
| 1 | "Am I slower than goal pace? Good." |
| 3 | Pace settled. Heart rate under control. |
| 5 | Take first gel (if fueling every 45 min from start) |
| 7 | Hydration check — have I taken at least 2 fluid stops? |
| 10 | Energy assessment: "Do I feel like I've run 5 miles?" If yes, pace is correct. |

**Coaching script:**
> "Miles 1–10 are not your race. They are the setup for your race. Your job is to protect your energy for mile 20. Every second you bank by going slow is worth three seconds at mile 22."

---

### Miles 10–20: Steady Execution

**Physical goals:**
- Lock into goal pace; this is the "automation" phase
- Nutrition discipline is non-negotiable (see Section 7)
- Begin small mental segmentation: "just get to mile 15," then "just get to mile 18"
- Watch for early signs of fatigue — any quad tightness or heaviness before mile 16 is a warning signal

**Mental goals:**
- Stay present — do not think about miles 20–26
- Find a runner running your pace and draft psychologically (not physically)
- Use mantras established in training

**Checkpoints:**

| Mile | Check |
|---|---|
| 10 | Gel taken. Fluid taken. Pace on target. |
| 13.1 | Split check — is half time within 30 seconds of plan? |
| 15 | Legs still feeling "available"? Good. If not, ease off 10 sec/mile now. |
| 17 | Gel taken. This is often the hardest mental stretch. |
| 18 | "I have 8 miles left. I have run 8 miles a hundred times." |
| 20 | Full assessment checkpoint (see below) |

**Mile 20 Assessment:**

| How You Feel | What It Means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Strong, legs available | Race is set up well | Maintain pace; consider pushing at mile 23 |
| Tired but in control | Normal marathon fatigue | Hold pace; do not push yet |
| Struggling, heavy legs | Wall is approaching | Drop to floor pace; shorten stride; focus on form |
| Significant pain or cramping | Something is wrong | Walk/run strategy; finish the race; do not force it |

**Coaching script:**
> "Miles 10–20 are where the race is won or lost — not by going fast, but by going consistently. Your job is to run the same mile over and over again. Boring is good. Boring means you're doing it right."

---

### Miles 20–26.2: Managing the Wall

**What the wall is:**
The wall (hitting glycogen depletion and central fatigue simultaneously) typically occurs between miles 18 and 22 for undertrained or poorly fueled runners. For well-prepared runners, it may appear as a general heaviness and mental fog rather than a dramatic breakdown.

**Physical goals:**
- Shorten stride, maintain cadence — this is more efficient than reaching
- Focus on arm drive — arms control legs when legs fail
- Accept that perceived effort will spike; that is normal and expected
- Do not stop to stretch — it rarely helps and wastes time and momentum

**Psychological strategies:**

1. **Segmentation:** Break the remaining miles into units of 2. "Just mile 20 and 21." Then reassess.
2. **Present tense anchoring:** Do not think about the finish line. Think about the next telephone pole, the next aid station, the next mile marker.
3. **Permission to slow slightly:** Tell the athlete in advance that it is okay to drop 10 sec/mile after mile 22 if needed. This prevents panic when it happens.
4. **Identity anchoring:** "You have done the training. This is just execution."
5. **Counting:** Count steps in groups of 100. It occupies the analytical mind.

**When to push:**

- After mile 23, if legs feel available, begin a controlled surge
- The final 5K should be a gradual acceleration — not a sprint
- Mile 25: begin racing. Everything left on the course.
- Mile 26: find whatever is left.

**Coaching script:**
> "The wall is real, but it is not a wall you crash into — it is a wall you run through. Your job after mile 20 is to shrink the race down to the smallest possible unit. The next aid station. The next mile marker. Do not run 6.2 miles. Run to mile 21. Then run to mile 22."

## 4. Aid Station Protocol

### General Principles

- Never skip a planned aid station in the second half of the race
- Walking through aid stations is not weakness — it is strategy
- Choking on fluids wastes more time than a clean 15-second stop

### When to Stop vs. Walk vs. Run Through

| Aid Station Location | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|
| Miles 1–8 | Run through; grab and go |
| Miles 9–18 | Walk 5–8 steps to drink; resume pace |
| Miles 19–26.2 | Walk every aid station; take everything available |

### How to Drink Without Losing Time

1. **Pinch the cup:** Pinch the top of the cup into a V-shape. This prevents splashing and allows controlled drinking while moving.
2. **Slow slightly, do not stop:** Reduce pace by 20–30 sec/mile for the 10 steps it takes to drink.
3. **Tilt head back slightly:** Prevents liquid from going down the windpipe.
4. **Take two cups if offered:** Water first to rinse, then sports drink for fuel (or vice versa based on preference established in training).
5. **Walk if you are behind on fluids:** A 10-second walk is worth it to get fluid in.

### Aid Station Timing

| Type | When to Take |
|---|---|
| Water | Every aid station from mile 6 onward |
| Sports drink (electrolytes) | Every other aid station, or as tolerated |
| Gel | Per fueling plan (every 40–45 min); take with water, not sports drink |
| Solid food (bananas, pretzels) | Miles 16+ if stomach is tolerating solids |
| Cola/caffeine | Miles 20+ as a psychological and mild stimulant boost |

## 5. Pre-Race Week and Morning Checklist

### Race Week (7 Days Out)

- [ ] Confirm bib pickup logistics and timing
- [ ] Lay out all race gear and do a dry run — put everything on
- [ ] Verify shoes are race-ready (not brand new, not worn out — 50–150 miles on them)
- [ ] Confirm fueling plan and verify all gels/chews are tested and available
- [ ] Begin slight carbohydrate increase (not a full carb-load binge — controlled increase)
- [ ] Hydrate consistently every day — urine should be pale yellow
- [ ] Do not try anything new: food, shoes, socks, compression gear
- [ ] Confirm wake time and transport logistics for race morning
- [ ] Sleep priority: focus on nights 5 and 4 before the race — night before is often poor

### Race Morning Checklist

**Gear:**
- [ ] Race bib pinned to singlet (4 pins, all corners)
- [ ] Timing chip on shoe (if separate from bib)
- [ ] GPS watch charged and set to correct distance/display
- [ ] Pace band on wrist or in pocket
- [ ] Shoes laced correctly (consider double-knot or race laces)
- [ ] Body Glide or Vaseline applied: inner thighs, underarms, nipples (men), toes if blister-prone
- [ ] Race belt or vest with gels loaded in order of consumption

**Nutrition:**
- [ ] Pre-race meal 2.5–3 hours before gun time (tested in training)
- [ ] Small snack 60–90 minutes before if long gap (half bagel, banana)
- [ ] 16–20 oz of water in the 2 hours before start; stop 45 minutes out
- [ ] Optional: 200mg caffeine 45–60 minutes before start (if trained with it)
- [ ] Gel consumed 15 minutes before gun if race nutrition starts at mile 4–5

**Morning Logistics:**
- [ ] Arrive at start area 60–75 minutes before gun
- [ ] Use port-a-potty at least 30 minutes before start (lines get long)
- [ ] Check gear bag and confirm drop procedure
- [ ] Locate correct corral
- [ ] Warm-up (see below)

### Race Morning Warm-Up

Recreational marathon runners should NOT do a traditional warm-up run. The race is long enough. Instead:

| Action | Timing Before Gun |
|---|---|
| Easy walk to start area | 20–30 min out |
| 5 min of dynamic drills: leg swings, hip circles, high knees | 15 min out |
| 4–6 strides of 20 seconds at easy pace | 10 min out |
| Stand in corral, shake out legs | Final 5 min |

## 6. Contingency Plans

### If It Is Hot (Temperature >65°F on Race Day)

**Decision tree:**
1. Acknowledge the condition before the gun — do not pretend it is fine
2. Adjust pace target to floor pace or slower
3. Take fluids at every single aid station without exception
4. Pour water on head, wrists, and neck at aid stations — evaporative cooling works
5. Abandon time goal completely above 75°F; the goal becomes finish safely
6. Watch for warning signs: goosebumps in heat, stopping sweating, confusion — these are emergencies

**Coaching script:**
> "Today is not a PR day. Today is a finish day. A disciplined finish in heat is worth more than a blown-up race chasing a time that isn't possible in these conditions. Run smart."

---

### If You Go Out Too Fast

**Detection:** Mile 1–3 pace is more than 20 seconds/mile faster than goal pace.

**Response protocol:**
1. Do not panic — one fast early mile is not catastrophic
2. Do not overcorrect by slowing dramatically — ease back to goal pace over 2–3 miles
3. Add 10 sec/mile to the target for the next 5 miles as insurance
4. Take next gel earlier than planned to stay ahead of glycogen demands
5. Accept that the time goal may need to shift to floor pace

**What not to do:** Do not "bank time" intentionally. Do not say "I'll slow down later." Slow down now.

---

### If You Hit the Wall Early (Before Mile 18)

**Detection:** Significant leg heaviness, pace dropping involuntarily, mental fog before mile 18.

**Response protocol:**
1. Drop pace to floor pace immediately — stop fighting the drop
2. Take a gel at the next aid station even if not on schedule
3. Take cola at the next aid station if available
4. Walk for 60 seconds, then resume running — walking resets perceived effort
5. Shorten stride and focus on form rather than speed
6. Mentally reframe the goal: "I am going to finish. I am going to run every step I can."

**What not to do:** Do not try to "push through" by maintaining pace when the body is sending distress signals. This strategy ends in a DNF or a medical tent.

---

### If You Are Having a Bad Day (No Specific Cause)

**Definition:** Everything feels hard early, motivation is low, the body just isn't responding.

**Response protocol:**
1. Acknowledge it without catastrophizing: "This is a bad day. Bad days still end at mile 26.2."
2. Reduce pace to floor pace by mile 5 — do not fight it past that point
3. Set micro-goals: make it to mile 10, then assess
4. Find one thing that is going right: breathing, posture, cadence
5. Use the crowd, signs, and spectators as fuel — let the race carry you

**Coaching script:**
> "Some days the body just doesn't show up. It happens to elite runners and it will happen to you eventually. Your job today is to finish with dignity and gather data for next time. A tough finish in bad conditions builds more fitness than a good finish on a good day."

## 7. Fueling and Hydration Race Plan

### Core Principles

1. Never try anything on race day that has not been tested in training
2. Fueling is preventive — you cannot recover glycogen once depleted
3. Stomach issues are the #1 cause of race blow-ups among recreational runners; train the gut

### Carbohydrate Targets

| Runner Weight | Minimum Carbs per Hour (Racing) | Target Carbs per Hour |
|---|---|---|
| <140 lbs | 45–50g | 55–60g |
| 140–180 lbs | 50–55g | 60–70g |
| >180 lbs | 55–60g | 65–75g |

Most energy gels contain 20–25g of carbohydrate. At one gel per 40–45 minutes, most runners are below optimal intake. Supplementing with sports drink at aid stations bridges the gap.

### Sample Fueling Timeline (4-Hour Marathon Target)

| Time | Mile (approx) | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Gun −15 min | — | Pre-race gel (optional; if gap since breakfast is >2.5 hrs) |
| 0:45 | ~6 | Gel #1 + water |
| 1:30 | ~11.5 | Gel #2 + water |
| 2:15 | ~17 | Gel #3 + water |
| 3:00 | ~22.5 | Gel #4 + water + cola if available |
| 3:30+ | ~25 | Final gel if tolerated |

Adjust the timeline to every 40 minutes if the athlete runs faster or has a history of GI issues earlier in races.

### Hydration Targets

| Phase | Target Intake |
|---|---|
| Miles 1–6 | 4–6 oz per aid station as tolerated |
| Miles 7–18 | 6–8 oz every aid station (water + sports drink alternating) |
| Miles 19–26.2 | Take everything; fluid priority over time |

**Warning:** Overhydration (hyponatremia) is a real risk for slower runners who drink too much plain water. Ensure electrolyte intake from sports drink or salt tabs alongside water.

### Sodium and Electrolytes

- Target 300–500mg sodium per hour during the race
- Most sports drinks provide 100–150mg per 8 oz serving
- Consider salt tabs (SaltStick, Precision Hydration) if sweating heavily, especially in heat
- Do not rely on food at aid stations for primary electrolyte needs

### What to Test in Training

- [ ] Test every gel flavor and brand planned for the race during long runs
- [ ] Practice taking gels while running at race pace (not during easy runs)
- [ ] Test sports drink brand served on the course (usually listed on race website)
- [ ] Simulate race fueling timing on at least 3 long runs
- [ ] Test caffeine-containing gels 4–6 weeks out (not first time on race day)
- [ ] Practice drinking from a cup while running

## 8. Athlete Race Strategy Briefings

### Two Weeks Out (14 Days Before Race)

**Goal:** Establish the race plan, set expectations, reduce anxiety.

**Topics to cover:**
1. Review training data and confirm pace band (build it together)
2. Walk through the course map — identify hills, turns, wind exposure
3. Discuss split strategy (even split with conservative start)
4. Confirm fueling and hydration plan
5. Address any outstanding concerns about fitness or readiness

**Coaching script:**
> "Two weeks out is the time to commit to a plan. Not a hope — a plan. Based on your training, here is what the data says you can do on race day in normal conditions. [Present pace band.] This is the ceiling. This is the floor. Your job is to run between these two lines for 26.2 miles. Let's talk through exactly how to do that."

**Key messages:**
- The fitness is locked in — training is done
- Taper is productive, not a sign of detraining
- Trust the training; race the plan

---

### One Week Out (7 Days Before Race)

**Goal:** Finalize logistics, lock in routine, reduce cognitive load.

**Topics to cover:**
1. Review and print the pace band
2. Confirm race morning logistics (wake time, transport, gear bag)
3. Review aid station protocol and fueling timing
4. Set expectations for taper madness (feeling heavy, anxious, doubting fitness)
5. Remind athlete: nothing new on race day

**Coaching script:**
> "This week is about one thing: protect the plan. Sleep, hydrate, eat normally — a little more carbs, nothing dramatic. Taper will make your legs feel weird and your mind feel anxious. That is normal. That is your body conserving energy. By race morning, you will feel ready. Trust that."

**What to finalize:**
- [ ] Pace band printed and ready
- [ ] Race gear fully assembled and tested
- [ ] Fueling supplies purchased and packed
- [ ] Race morning schedule written down with buffer time

---

### The Night Before (Race Eve)

**Goal:** Calm nerves, confirm execution, set the tone.

**Keep it short.** The athlete does not need new information the night before. They need confidence and clarity.

**Coaching script:**
> "Here is all you need to remember tomorrow. Start slower than you want to. Take your gels on schedule. Walk through aid stations in the second half. When it gets hard after mile 20, shorten your stride and focus on your arms. You have done the work. Tomorrow is just the celebration."

**Three reminders only:**
1. Start 10 seconds per mile slower than goal pace for the first 3 miles
2. Gels on schedule, no skipping
3. Mile 20–26.2: run one mile at a time

**What not to do the night before:**
- Do not review training data or compare to other runners
- Do not change the nutrition or hydration plan
- Do not set a new goal based on feeling "surprisingly good" that morning
- Do not stay up late — even if sleep is poor, lying down rests the body

## 9. Post-Race Analysis Framework

### Capture Window

The best post-race data is captured within **24–48 hours** of finishing. After 72 hours, recall degrades significantly. Prompt the athlete immediately upon finishing.

### What to Capture While Fresh

**Objective data (from GPS watch and race timing):**
- [ ] Official finish time and chip time
- [ ] Half split and full split
- [ ] Per-mile pace (download from watch)
- [ ] Heart rate data by mile if available
- [ ] Elevation-corrected pace if applicable

**Subjective data (athlete self-report):**

| Category | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| Pacing | At what mile did pace first feel difficult? When did pace drop involuntarily? |
| Fueling | How many gels taken? Any GI issues? When? |
| Hydration | Did you feel thirsty at any point? Cramping? |
| Mental | What was the hardest mile mentally? What helped? What would you change? |
| Physical | Any pain during the race (not DOMS)? Blister or chafing issues? |
| Energy | Did you have anything left at mile 25? Did you fade or hold? |

### Interpretation Guidelines

| Observation | Likely Cause | Next Cycle Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Positive split >5 min | Too fast in first half | Race strategy discipline; more pace-specific long runs |
| Wall before mile 20 | Undertrained or underfueled | Long run volume; gut training; fueling compliance |
| Strong negative split | Conservative start; well-trained | Consider more aggressive goal next cycle |
| GI issues mid-race | Fueling not trained in long runs | Gut training protocol; practice race-pace nutrition |
| Strong miles 20–23, fade 23–26 | Mild glycogen depletion | Add late-race gel; more miles 22+ in training |
| DNF or significant slowdown | Multiple possible causes | Full debrief; distinguish fitness, strategy, or health issue |

### Post-Race Recovery Note to Athlete

Include a brief acknowledgment regardless of outcome:

> "Whatever happened out there today — whether it went exactly to plan or fell apart at mile 18 — you did the work and you showed up. We will look at the data together and use it to build a smarter plan for next time. Right now, rest, eat, and let the body recover. Analysis can wait 48 hours. Recovery cannot."
