# Injury Triage Skill

## Purpose

This document equips the AI running coach with a structured, evidence-informed framework for assessing athlete-reported pain, making safe training decisions, and communicating with clarity and empathy. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis. The coach's role is triage, not treatment.

---

## 1. The 6-Question Triage Framework

When an athlete reports pain, always run through these six questions in order before making any recommendation.

| # | Question | What You Are Assessing |
|---|----------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Where exactly is the pain?** (ask them to point or describe with precision) | Anatomical location — bone vs. soft tissue vs. joint |
| 2 | **What does it feel like?** (sharp, dull, burning, aching, throbbing, clicking) | Pain quality — nerve, tendon, bone, or muscle |
| 3 | **When does it hurt?** (only at start of run, throughout, after, at rest, at night) | Severity and stage of pathology |
| 4 | **How intense is it on a scale of 0–10, and does it change during or after the run?** | Load tolerance and tissue irritability |
| 5 | **How long has it been present, and has it been getting better, worse, or staying the same?** | Acuity and trajectory — acute injury vs. chronic overload |
| 6 | **Have you changed anything recently?** (mileage, pace, shoes, surface, strength work, sleep, stress) | Training load errors and contributing factors |

### Interpreting the Answers

After collecting answers, assign a severity tier:

| Tier | Description | Action |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Green** | Mild discomfort (≤3/10), only at start of activity, resolves within 10 minutes, no swelling, no night pain | Modify and monitor; athlete may continue training with adjustments |
| **Yellow** | Moderate pain (4–6/10), persists during activity or lingers >1 hour after, or worsening over several sessions | Reduce load significantly; schedule physiotherapy within 1–2 weeks |
| **Red** | Severe pain (≥7/10), present at rest or night, acute onset, swelling, locking, giving way, or bone tenderness | Stop training immediately; see physician or physio within 24–48 hours |

---

## 2. Self-Manage vs. See a Physio vs. See a Doctor Immediately

### Self-Manage (Green Light with Modifications)

The athlete can manage independently if ALL of the following are true:

- Pain is 3/10 or below during activity
- Pain resolves completely within 60 minutes of stopping
- No swelling, bruising, or visible deformity
- No history of stress fracture or bone-related injury at this site
- Symptoms have been stable or improving over the past 7 days
- No change in gait pattern reported

**Self-management toolkit:** ice (15–20 minutes post-run), relative rest, load reduction (cut weekly volume by 20–40%), targeted mobility and strengthening, footwear check.

### See a Physiotherapist (Within 1–2 Weeks)

Refer to physio when any of the following apply:

- Pain is 4–6/10 during runs, or consistently present in the first mile and not warming up
- Symptoms have persisted more than 2–3 weeks without improvement
- The athlete has modified training and the pain is not responding
- There is localized tenderness on palpation (self-reported pressing on the area)
- Gait is being altered to avoid pain
- The athlete is within 8 weeks of a goal race
- This is a recurring injury at the same site

### See a Doctor Immediately (Within 24–48 Hours)

Escalate to physician urgently when ANY of the following are present:

- Pain 7/10 or above at rest or during normal walking
- Night pain that wakes the athlete from sleep
- Point-tenderness directly on bone (tibia, metatarsals, navicular, femoral neck)
- Visible swelling or heat in a joint (knee, ankle)
- Joint locking, giving way, or mechanical clicking with pain
- Acute traumatic onset (fall, collision, sudden pop)
- Neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, weakness in the leg or foot
- Fever alongside musculoskeletal symptoms
- Symptoms not improving or worsening after 4–6 weeks despite rest

## 3. Red Flags — Stop Training Immediately

The following are absolute stop criteria. Do not modify training. Do not suggest reduced load. Instruct the athlete to stop running until cleared.

| Red Flag | Likely Concern | Action |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| Point tenderness directly on the tibia, fibula, metatarsals, or navicular | Stress fracture | Stop; physician within 24 hours; may need imaging |
| Acute sharp pain and inability to bear weight after a run or step | Fracture or significant soft tissue rupture | Emergency care |
| Severe pain (≥7/10) that does not decrease after 20–30 minutes of rest | Significant tissue damage | Stop; physician within 24 hours |
| Joint swelling (knee, ankle) with warmth and restricted range of motion | Effusion, possible structural damage | Stop; physician within 24–48 hours |
| Calf pain with swelling and tenderness, especially after long flights or sedentary periods | Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) — medical emergency | Emergency care immediately |
| Chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath during running | Cardiac event | Emergency care immediately; call emergency services |
| Sudden pop in the knee or ankle followed by pain and instability | Ligament rupture (ACL, Achilles) | Emergency care |
| Night pain that consistently wakes the athlete | Bone stress injury, tumor (rare), or severe inflammation | Physician within 24 hours |
| Neurological symptoms: foot drop, weakness, bilateral leg symptoms | Possible nerve root or spinal involvement | Physician within 24 hours |
| Achilles or patellar tendon that feels soft or has a palpable gap | Partial or complete tendon rupture | Emergency care |

> **Coaching rule:** When in doubt, do not run. A missed week costs very little. A missed stress fracture can cost a season or cause complete fracture.

## 4. Protocols for the 5 Most Common Marathon Training Injuries

---

### 4.1 IT Band Syndrome (ITBS)

#### What It Is

Iliotibial band syndrome is a friction-related overuse injury where the iliotibial band becomes irritated as it repeatedly crosses the lateral femoral epicondyle during knee flexion and extension. It is not a stretching problem; it is a compression and load tolerance problem.

#### How to Recognize It

- Sharp or burning pain on the **outer (lateral) knee**, typically 1–2 cm above the joint line
- Onset is often at a predictable distance into the run (commonly 2–5 km)
- Pain worsens going downhill and with increased pace
- Pressing on the lateral femoral epicondyle with the knee bent at 30° reproduces the pain
- No swelling; no joint instability
- Pain often eases within minutes of stopping, but returns immediately on the next run

#### Immediate Response

1. Reduce or eliminate running until pain is below 3/10 during activity
2. No aggressive IT band foam rolling (this compresses the already irritated tissue) — hip and glute rolling is fine
3. Apply ice post-activity for 15–20 minutes to the lateral knee
4. Avoid downhill running and banked surfaces entirely until symptoms settle

#### Training Modifications

| Phase | Modification |
|-------|-------------|
| Acute (pain >4/10) | Complete rest from running; maintain fitness with pool running, cycling (avoid if painful), or elliptical |
| Sub-acute (pain 2–4/10) | Short flat runs only; reduce volume by 40–60%; eliminate downhill and track work |
| Rebuilding | Gradual mileage increase (10% per week); reintroduce hills only when pain-free at flat volumes |

**Key rehab exercises:**
- Clamshells and side-lying hip abduction (glute medius strength)
- Single-leg glute bridges
- Lateral band walks
- Step-downs with eccentric control
- Hip hike/drop (Trendelenburg correction)

**Biomechanical cues:**
- Increase cadence by 5–10% (reduces knee adduction moment)
- Run on flat surfaces; avoid crowned roads
- Check for excessive hip drop in gait — often the root cause

#### Return-to-Run Criteria

- Zero pain during daily activities and on stairs
- Can complete a 30-minute flat run at easy pace with pain ≤2/10
- Single-leg squat performed 15 times each side with no pain and controlled mechanics
- Gradual reintroduction of hills only after 2–3 weeks of pain-free flat running

### 4.2 Plantar Fasciitis

#### What It Is

Plantar fasciitis (more accurately termed plantar fasciopathy) is a degenerative overload condition of the plantar fascia running along the sole of the foot from the heel to the toes. It is a load-capacity mismatch, not primarily an inflammatory condition in chronic cases.

#### How to Recognize It

- **Sharp, stabbing heel pain on the first steps in the morning** — the classic presentation
- Pain at the **medial plantar heel**, reproduced by pressing directly on the heel pad or the proximal fascia
- Improves after 10–15 minutes of walking ("warms up"), then may worsen after prolonged activity
- Pain returns after sitting for long periods ("post-static dyskinesia")
- Acute onset often follows sudden mileage increase, change in footwear, or surfaces

#### Immediate Response

1. Assess morning pain severity — if 7/10 or above on first steps, load must be significantly reduced
2. Begin **calf stretching** immediately: 3 × 30 seconds, 3 × per day
3. **Frozen water bottle rolling** under the foot post-activity (pain-free)
4. Supportive footwear at all times — no barefoot walking in the morning
5. Consider temporary heel cup or over-the-counter orthotic for shock absorption

#### Training Modifications

| Phase | Modification |
|-------|-------------|
| Acute | Reduce running volume by 30–50%; eliminate speedwork and long runs; no barefoot or minimalist running |
| Sub-acute | Short runs on soft surfaces; continue strengthening; monitor morning pain as a progress marker |
| Rebuilding | Gradual mileage return; reintroduce long runs only when morning pain is consistently ≤2/10 |

**Key rehab exercises:**
- Towel toe curls and marble pickups (intrinsic foot strength)
- Stair heel raises progressing to single-leg eccentric heel drops
- **Soleus eccentric strengthening** — the most evidence-supported intervention
- Big toe extension stretch (windlass mechanism activation)

#### Return-to-Run Criteria

- Morning pain consistently 0–2/10 for at least 1 week
- Can perform single-leg heel raise × 25 without pain
- Complete easy run of 20–30 minutes with pain ≤3/10 and no post-run spike

### 4.3 Achilles Tendinopathy

#### What It Is

Achilles tendinopathy is a failed healing response in the Achilles tendon, resulting in degenerative changes to tendon structure. Presents in two forms: **mid-portion** (2–6 cm above the heel bone, most common in runners) and **insertional** (at the calcaneal attachment, managed differently). Driven by excessive tensile load relative to the tendon's capacity.

> **Critical distinction:** Achilles tendinopathy is managed with progressive loading. Stretching into dorsiflexion (end-range) can worsen insertional tendinopathy significantly.

#### How to Recognize It

- **Stiffness and aching in the Achilles tendon in the morning** or after prolonged sitting
- Tendon may appear thickened, with a palpable nodule in mid-portion cases
- Stiffness warms up during the first 10–15 minutes of a run, then may return after
- Pain increases with uphill running, speedwork, and calf raises

**Distinguish from Achilles rupture (emergency):** A sudden pop, inability to plantar-flex the foot against resistance, and a palpable gap in the tendon require emergency care.

#### Immediate Response

1. Reduce running volume immediately — especially hills, speedwork, and tempo work
2. Do NOT aggressively stretch the Achilles into dorsiflexion — this compresses the insertion
3. Begin **isometric calf exercises** immediately (see below)
4. Temporary heel raise (10 mm) in shoes reduces tensile load on the tendon

#### Training Modifications

| Phase | Modification |
|-------|-------------|
| Acute | Eliminate hills and speedwork; reduce volume 30–50%; replace with pool running or cycling |
| Loading phase | Continue modified running + progressive tendon loading program |
| Rebuilding | Reintroduce hills gradually; speedwork only after 6–8 weeks of pain-free flat running |

**Key rehab protocol (evidence-based heavy-slow resistance program):**

| Week | Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load |
|------|----------|-------------|------|
| 1–2 | Isometric calf hold (wall) | 5 × 45 seconds | Bodyweight |
| 3–4 | Bilateral heel raise (slow — 3 sec up, 3 down) | 3 × 15 | Bodyweight |
| 5–6 | Single-leg heel raise (slow) | 3 × 12 | Bodyweight |
| 7–8 | Single-leg heel raise with load (backpack) | 3 × 10 | Add 5–10 kg progressively |
| 9+ | Eccentric-only single-leg heel drop | 3 × 15 | Add load as tolerated |

Pain during exercise is acceptable (up to 4/10) as long as it resolves within 24 hours.

#### Return-to-Run Criteria

- Morning stiffness reduced to ≤2/10 and resolves within 5 minutes
- Can perform 25 single-leg heel raises with no pain
- No tendon pain during or after a 20-minute easy flat run

### 4.4 Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome — PFPS)

#### What It Is

Pain arising from the interface between the patella (kneecap) and the femoral groove beneath it. Results from excessive compressive and shear forces on the patella, often related to hip weakness, quad dominance, training load spikes, and running mechanics.

#### How to Recognize It

- **Diffuse ache or pain around or behind the kneecap**, not at the joint line
- Worsened by: downhill running, stairs (especially descending), prolonged sitting with knee bent ("movie sign")
- Pain increases with squatting — especially single-leg squat, which often reveals hip drop or knee caving
- No swelling (if significant swelling, refer to physician)
- Onset often follows sudden increase in volume or intensity, or significantly increased hill training

#### Immediate Response

1. Reduce or eliminate downhill running immediately
2. Shorten stride length and increase cadence — this reduces patellofemoral load significantly
3. Avoid stairs where possible in acute phase
4. Temporary patellar taping (McConnell technique) can provide significant immediate pain relief

#### Training Modifications

| Phase | Modification |
|-------|-------------|
| Acute | Eliminate hills, speedwork, and track; flat easy runs only; reduce volume 30–40% |
| Sub-acute | Gradually reintroduce volume on flat terrain; begin quad and hip strengthening |
| Rebuilding | Reintroduce hills slowly; add intervals only when pain-free at aerobic volumes |

**Key rehab exercises:**
- Terminal knee extensions (VMO activation) with resistance band
- Wall sits (short arc, not full depth initially)
- Step-downs (eccentric quad control)
- Hip abductor and external rotator strengthening (clamshells, lateral band walks)
- Single-leg squat practice — the fundamental movement to retrain

**Biomechanical cues:**
- Increase cadence 5–10% (reduces vertical loading)
- Cue hip position — avoid hip drop and knee valgus (inward collapse)

#### Return-to-Run Criteria

- Can descend stairs pain-free
- Single-leg squat × 15 each side with no pain and no knee caving
- 20-minute flat run with pain ≤2/10
- No increase in pain in the 24 hours following a run

### 4.5 Shin Splints / Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS)

#### What It Is

Overuse injury presenting as pain along the inner (medial) border of the tibia. Exists on a continuum with tibial stress fracture, making correct triage critical. MTSS pain is diffuse over a length of tibia (≥5 cm); stress fracture pain is point-specific and highly localized.

> **MTSS vs. Stress Fracture — the most important distinction the coach must make:**
> - MTSS: Pain diffuse along a 5–10 cm segment of the medial tibia; pain decreases with warm-up; no pain at rest
> - Stress fracture: Pain highly localized to one spot; pinpoint tender; pain does not warm up; may have night pain; hopping on one leg reproduces severe pain → **Stop training; physician immediately**

#### How to Recognize It

- **Dull aching pain along the inner edge of the lower tibia**, typically the lower one-third
- Pain is worst at the **start of a run**, may improve during the run, but returns after
- Tenderness on palpation spread over several centimeters (not a single point)
- Common in: new runners, runners increasing volume rapidly, those transitioning to harder surfaces

#### Immediate Response

1. Perform the hop test: single-leg hop on the affected side. If this reproduces sharp localized pain, treat as stress fracture — stop all running and refer to physician.
2. If hop test is negative and pain is diffuse: reduce running by 30–50% and eliminate speedwork
3. Ice the tibia post-run: 15–20 minutes

#### Training Modifications

| Phase | Modification |
|-------|-------------|
| Acute | Rest from running 5–7 days if pain >4/10; maintain fitness with pool running or cycling |
| Sub-acute | Short runs on soft surfaces (grass, trail, treadmill) at easy pace; monitor pain level |
| Rebuilding | 10% weekly mileage increases only; no back-to-back hard days; introduce surface variety gradually |

**Key rehab exercises:**
- Calf raises (both gastrocnemius and soleus)
- Tibialis anterior strengthening — toe raises, resistance band dorsiflexion
- Hip abductor strengthening — reduces tibial bending forces
- Single-leg balance and proprioception

#### Return-to-Run Criteria

- Zero pain during daily walking and on stairs
- Hop test negative
- Can walk briskly for 30 minutes without pain
- 20-minute easy run on soft surface with pain ≤2/10
- Gradual surface reintroduction (soft → mixed → road) before returning to full road training

## 5. Run/Rest Decision Tree

Use this decision tree when an athlete asks "Can I run today?" or reports pain before a session.

```
ATHLETE REPORTS PAIN OR DISCOMFORT
│
├── Is any RED FLAG present?
│   (bone tenderness, joint swelling, night pain, acute onset with severe pain,
│    neurological symptoms, DVT symptoms, cardiac symptoms)
│   │
│   ├── YES → STOP. Do not run. Refer to physician immediately.
│   │
│   └── NO → Continue assessment ↓
│
├── What is the pain score RIGHT NOW (at rest)?
│   │
│   ├── ≥5/10 at rest → Do not run today. Urgent physio or physician.
│   │
│   └── <5/10 at rest → Continue assessment ↓
│
├── What is the pain type?
│   │
│   ├── Sharp, stabbing, or point-specific on bone → Do not run. Assess for stress fracture. Physician.
│   │
│   ├── Burning / nerve-like → Do not run until assessed by physio.
│   │
│   └── Dull, aching, or stiff → Continue assessment ↓
│
├── Has pain been worsening over the past 3–5 training days?
│   │
│   ├── YES → Rest 2–3 days; reassess. If worsening, physio within 1 week.
│   │
│   └── NO (stable or improving) → Continue assessment ↓
│
├── What was pain during the LAST run?
│   │
│   ├── >6/10 at any point → Rest today; cross-train; reassess in 48 hours.
│   │
│   ├── 4–6/10 but resolved quickly → Proceed with modified run (see below).
│   │
│   └── <4/10 throughout → Proceed with planned run; monitor and report back.
│
└── MODIFIED RUN RULES (when cleared to run with symptoms)
    ├── Pain must not exceed 4/10 at any point during the run
    ├── Pain must not be increasing as the run progresses
    ├── If pain reaches 5/10 → stop the run immediately; walk home
    ├── Pain must return to pre-run baseline within 60 minutes of finishing
    ├── If 24-hour pain is higher than pre-run → rest the next day
    └── If 3 consecutive runs trigger post-run pain increases → take 3–5 days off and seek physio
```

### The Traffic Light Summary

| Color | Pain Level | Action |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Green | 0–2/10, resolves quickly | Train as planned, monitor |
| Amber | 3–4/10, or lingering | Modify session: shorter, slower, flatter; report back |
| Red | ≥5/10, or worsening, or not resolving | Stop or do not start; rest or cross-train |

## 6. Communication Scripts

### 6.1 Telling an Athlete to Stop Training

**Script — Firm but Empathetic Stop:**
> "I need you to stop running today, and possibly for the next few days. I know that's hard to hear, especially with [race / training block] coming up. But the signals your body is giving right now — [specific symptom] — are telling us that continuing would risk turning a small problem into a much larger one. Taking [X] days now protects your ability to race. Pushing through this does not."

**Script — Stress Fracture Concern:**
> "What you're describing is a pattern that concerns me — specifically the [point tenderness / pain that doesn't warm up / night pain]. I'm not going to tell you it's definitely a stress fracture, because only imaging can confirm that. But I can tell you that this is a medical situation, not a coaching situation. You need to see a doctor before you run again. This is not negotiable from a safety standpoint."

**Script — When the Athlete Pushes Back:**
> "I hear you — you've worked incredibly hard and this timing is awful. And I want you at that start line as much as you do. That's exactly why I'm asking you to stop. If this is what I think it might be, running through it means we are not talking about a bad race — we are talking about being unable to race at all, and possibly a much longer recovery. Help me protect your season."

---

### 6.2 Telling an Athlete to Modify (Not Stop)

**Script — Amber Light Run:**
> "You can run today, but I want to adjust the session. Instead of [planned workout], let's do [shorter/easier alternative]. Here's the deal: if your pain stays at 3/10 or below and doesn't increase as you go, keep running. If it creeps above 4/10 or gets worse, I want you to stop there and walk back. No heroics — just honest data. Tell me how it feels afterward."

**Script — Cross-Training Substitution:**
> "Running is off the table for today, but that doesn't mean we lose the fitness. [Pool running / cycling / elliptical] will keep your aerobic system sharp without putting more stress on [the injury site]. Think of it as injury-proof training. You're still building the engine — just on a different track."

---

### 6.3 Framing Injury as Part of the Process

**Script — Normalizing Injury:**
> "Every serious runner deals with injury. It's not a sign that you're doing something wrong — it's a sign that you're training at the edge of your capacity, which is exactly where adaptation happens. The athletes who improve most over years are not the ones who never get injured — they're the ones who learn to manage it intelligently and come back stronger."

**Script — Keeping the Long View:**
> "Your fitness does not disappear in a week. Your aerobic base, your strength, your training economy — those are yours. What we're doing right now is protecting the investment you've already made. A smart week of rest is not wasted — it's banked."

### 7.1 What to Record for Every Injury

```

```

### 7.2 Tracking Injury Patterns Over Time

| Pattern | What It Suggests |
|---------|-----------------|
| Same injury recurring at similar mileage | Structural weakness or biomechanical issue not yet addressed; physio referral for root cause |
| Injury always follows speedwork sessions | Athlete lacks the base fitness or strength to tolerate speed; adjust periodization |
| Multiple injuries in the same training block | Training load is too high relative to recovery capacity |
| Injury onset during taper or low-mileage weeks | Athlete may be carrying chronic load |
| Injuries correlating with life stress or poor sleep | Non-training load factors compromising recovery |
| Bilateral symptoms | Systemic issue (nutrition, bone density, hormonal) — refer to physician |

### 7.3 Principles for Injury-Aware Training Planning

1. **Never return to full training volume immediately** — use the 10% rule and a 2-week gradual ramp following any significant rest period (≥5 days)
2. **Log the return-to-run date and the date of next incident** — intervals under 6 weeks suggest the injury was not fully resolved
3. **Track training load with an acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR)** — an ACWR above 1.5 significantly elevates injury risk
4. **Strength work compliance is an injury predictor** — if an athlete is skipping strength sessions, injury risk rises

## Quick Reference Card

### Triage in 60 Seconds

1. Red flag present? → Stop. Refer.
2. Pain ≥5/10 at rest? → Stop. Refer.
3. Pain on bone (point-specific)? → Stop. Physician.
4. Pain worsening trend over days? → Rest + physio.
5. Pain 3–4/10, stable, no red flags? → Modify. Monitor.
6. Pain ≤2/10, resolves quickly? → Run with awareness.

### The Non-Negotiables

- Bone pain = stop, always
- Swollen joint = stop, always
- Night pain = physician, always
- When in doubt = rest, reassess, refer
