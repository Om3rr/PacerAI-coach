# Athlete Assessment Skill
> For returning marathon runners and second-marathon trainees

---

## Overview

This document guides a coach through the full intake and assessment process for a runner returning to marathon training — someone who has finished at least one marathon and is now preparing for their second. The goal is to build a precise, honest picture of the athlete's current state, not their aspirational state, and to produce a training plan anchored in reality.

The frameworks drawn on here — Daniels' Running Formula, Hansons Marathon Method, 80/20 Running, Pfitzinger's Advanced Marathoning, and sports psychology research — often disagree on specifics. Where they conflict, the coaching principle is: **err on the side of the athlete finishing healthy and improving, not hitting a time**.

---

## 1. Initial Intake Questions

### 1.1 Running History

- How long have you been running consistently (not counting gaps)?
- What was your peak weekly mileage in the 12 months before your last marathon?
- What did your training look like — structured plan, self-coached, running club?
- Did you complete your last marathon training block without major interruption?
- How did your first marathon go? What happened in the last 6 miles?
- What was your finish time, and how did you feel at mile 18, mile 22, and the finish?
- Have you raced any distances since your last marathon? What were those times?
- What is your current weekly mileage, and how consistent has it been over the last 8–12 weeks?

**Why the last 6 miles matter:** Whether the athlete held pace, faded moderately, or collapsed tells the coach more than the finish time alone. A 4:10 runner who negative-split the second half is a very different athlete than a 3:55 runner who walked miles 22–26.

### 1.2 Injury History

- Have you had any injuries in the last two years that caused you to stop running for more than one week?
- List each: what was it, how long were you out, how was it treated, and are you fully recovered?
- Do you have any chronic or recurring issues — IT band, plantar fascia, achilles, knee, hip flexor, shin splints?
- Have you ever had a stress fracture or stress reaction? Where, and how long ago?
- Do any issues currently limit your training — pain, stiffness, tightness that you "run through"?
- Are you currently seeing a physio, sports doctor, or other practitioner?
- What shoes do you train in, and how old are they?

**Interpretation:** Any stress fracture history warrants a conservative build and mandatory cross-training weeks. Chronic soft tissue issues that are not resolved are a near-certain injury trigger when mileage increases. Do not proceed to a standard plan until a physio has cleared recurring issues.

### 1.3 Life Context

- What does your week look like — work hours, commute, family responsibilities?
- Do you have children, and if so, what are their ages and care demands?
- What is your sleep situation like — hours per night, quality, consistency?
- Is there anything major happening in your life over the next 16–20 weeks (travel, house move, new job, family events)?
- Who supports your training at home? Is it a source of friction or support?
- How do you handle stress generally — do you find running helps, or does stress affect your motivation and recovery?

### 1.4 Schedule and Logistics

- How many days per week can you realistically run?
- What days are hardest to fit in training?
- Do you have access to a track, trails, hills?
- What time of day do you usually run?
- Can you do a long run of 2.5–3.5 hours on weekends without major life disruption?
- Do you have access to a gym, pool, or bike for cross-training?
- What is your race date, and is it fixed?

### 1.5 Goals

**This section is deliberately last.** Hearing the goal before the history causes the coach to anchor on it. The goal should be formed after the data, not before.

- What is your goal for this marathon?
- Why that goal? Where does the number come from?
- What would "success" look like if you don't hit the time goal?
- Have you run any tune-up races recently?
- Are you open to adjusting your goal during training if your fitness data suggests a different target?

The last question is diagnostic. Athletes who resist adjusting a goal on the basis of evidence are at higher injury and burnout risk.

## 2. Fitness Assessment Methods

Never plan from a goal. Plan from current fitness.

### 2.1 Recent Race Times

Recent = within the last 8–12 weeks, at genuine effort on a standard course. A half marathon is the gold standard predictor for marathon training paces; a 10K is acceptable. A 5K alone is insufficient.

Use Daniels' VDOT tables as the primary conversion tool. Apply a modest conservatism factor: derive marathon goal pace from one VDOT point below the race-derived value. This accounts for the fact that most athletes at this level have not yet fully developed the fat-oxidation and muscular endurance required to sustain race-derived training paces for 26.2 miles.

**Example:** A runner with a recent half marathon of 1:52 has a VDOT of approximately 44. Derive training paces from VDOT 43, with marathon goal pace at the slower end of the VDOT 44 range. Revisit after 8 weeks.

**Caution:** Race times from more than 16 weeks ago, from very hilly courses, or from races run in extreme heat should be treated as loose approximations only.

### 2.2 Time Trials

If no recent race data exists:

**10K time trial:** Preferred option. Warm up thoroughly. Run a genuine but controlled 10K on a flat, measurable course. Maps cleanly to VDOT.

**5K time trial:** More accessible, less predictive for marathon-specific fitness. Apply one additional VDOT conservatism point.

**Avoid:** Self-reported "comfortable pace" estimates, GPS pace data from casual runs, and "I feel like I'm in shape to run X" assessments. These are systematically optimistic.

### 2.3 Heart Rate-Based Assessment

**Resting heart rate:** Measure on waking, before standing, for three consecutive mornings. A resting HR 5–8 bpm above the athlete's normal baseline during training is a useful early warning of overtraining or illness.

**Aerobic threshold assessment (MAF method):** Have the athlete run for 30–45 minutes at approximately 180 minus age (adjusted for training history) and record pace. This establishes a baseline for aerobic efficiency that can be retested every 4–6 weeks.

**Lactate threshold from HR:** A 30-minute time trial at maximum sustainable effort; the average HR during the final 20 minutes approximates lactate threshold HR (Friel method).

**Limitations:** HR is highly sensitive to sleep deprivation, caffeine, heat, humidity, and emotional stress. Patterns over 3–4 weeks are meaningful; single data points are not.

### 2.4 Training Log Analysis

If the athlete has trained consistently for 8+ weeks before assessment, their log is itself evidence of fitness:
- What is the longest run completed, and at what pace and effort?
- What is the average weekly mileage over the last 4 weeks?
- What is the progression of easy run pace at a given HR over the last 8 weeks?
- Has the athlete done any structured workouts, and what were the results?

## 3. Setting a Realistic Marathon Goal Pace

### 3.1 The Principle: Train Who Shows Up

The most common coaching error is building a plan around who the athlete intends to be rather than who they are on assessment day.

**The process:**
1. Establish current VDOT from best available data
2. Derive all training paces from this VDOT — easy, marathon, threshold, interval, repetition
3. Set the marathon race goal at the pace associated with this VDOT
4. Build in explicit reassessment points at weeks 6 and 12. Adjust then — not on day one.

### 3.2 The Hansons Check

Hansons Marathon Method treats marathon goal pace (MP) as the anchor of the training plan from week one. If the athlete cannot sustain MP in training over 10–12 miles, the goal is wrong.

### 3.3 The 80/20 Check

If the goal pace requires threshold and MP workouts at efforts that leave the athlete unable to complete easy days easily, the goal pace is too aggressive for their current fitness.

A simple check: after a threshold or MP workout, the athlete should be able to run the next day at easy effort without major fatigue accumulation over the week.

### 3.4 Adjustments for Course and Conditions

- **Elevation gain:** Add approximately 10–15 seconds per mile for every 1,000 feet of net gain per marathon
- **Heat and humidity:** For every 10°F above 55°F on race day, expect a 1–3% performance decline

## 4. Life-Stress Profile

### 4.1 Why Life Stress Is Training Stress

The physiological literature is unambiguous: psychological and emotional stress activates the same HPA axis stress response as physical training load. Cortisol is catabolic — it breaks down tissue. Chronically elevated cortisol from work, relationship, or financial stress impairs recovery in the same way that chronic overtraining does.

### 4.2 The Stress Audit

Score each domain on a 1–5 scale (1 = minimal stress, 5 = high/unmanageable):

| Domain | Score (1–5) |
|---|---|
| Work demands and pressure | |
| Relationship quality and stability | |
| Financial situation | |
| Parenting / caregiving demands | |
| Sleep quality and quantity | |
| Nutrition and eating patterns | |
| Social support and isolation | |
| Major life transitions (moving, new job, loss) | |

**Interpretation:**
- Total 8–16: Low stress. Standard training volume is appropriate.
- Total 17–24: Moderate stress. Build conservatively; recovery weeks every 3rd week rather than every 4th.
- Total 25–32: High stress. Reduce target peak mileage by 15–20%; prioritize sleep over optional workouts.
- Total 33–40: Very high stress. Strongly advise "finish healthy" goal rather than time goal, or postpone.

### 4.3 Sleep as the Primary Recovery Variable

At intake, ask:
- How many hours of sleep do you average per night?
- Do you wake during the night? How often?
- Do you feel rested when you wake?
- Do you use an alarm on training days?

A runner consistently getting under 7 hours should treat sleep as the first training variable to optimize — before adding mileage, before adding a workout day, before anything else.

### 4.4 Integrating Stress into the Plan

Write explicit decision rules into the plan:
- **Weekly check-in:** "On a scale of 1–10, how recovered do you feel going into this week's key workouts?"
- **Score under 6:** Downgrade the key workout (threshold becomes steady-state; long run shortens 2–3 miles)
- **Score 4 or below:** Replace key workouts with easy running only. Treat as unplanned recovery week.
- **Sleep below 6 hours two nights in a row:** Automatic downgrade of the next day's workout intensity.

These rules should be written into the plan at the outset, not added reactively.

## 5. Red Flags in Athlete History

### 5.1 Structural Red Flags

**History of stress fracture:**
Any stress fracture in the lower extremity demands a conservative approach. Include mandatory cross-training weeks, a slower mileage build, and no back-to-back days of high impact in the early phase.

**Femoral neck stress fracture specifically:**
This injury carries risk of displaced fracture and avascular necrosis. Requires physician clearance before beginning the plan.

**Recurring soft tissue injury in the same location:**
IT band syndrome that recurred in three consecutive training cycles is a biomechanical or structural issue that has not been addressed. Require physio assessment before proceeding.

**Chronic pain during running:**
Any pain the athlete describes as "I just run through it" should be assessed before training begins, not during.

### 5.2 Physiological Red Flags

**Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S):**
Red flags include:
- History of stress fractures (especially multiple)
- Menstrual irregularity or loss of period in female athletes
- Preoccupation with weight and food, or significant weight loss
- Fatigue and poor recovery disproportionate to training load

If RED-S is suspected, referral to a sports dietitian and sports medicine physician is mandatory before proceeding.

**Anemia or low ferritin:**
A runner who is chronically fatigued, whose HR is elevated relative to pace, and whose pace has declined despite consistent training should be tested. Serum ferritin below 35 ng/mL is associated with impaired performance even in the absence of clinical anemia.

**Cardiac symptoms:**
Any history of chest pain during exercise, palpitations, syncope, or family history of sudden cardiac death in a first-degree relative under 50 requires medical clearance. Do not proceed without it.

### 5.3 Psychological Red Flags

**Obsessive goal attachment:** An athlete who cannot engage with conversations about adjusting their goal is at elevated psychological risk. Name this gently and directly.

**History of overtraining syndrome:** An athlete who has experienced OTS is at elevated risk of recurrence. The plan should have a lower mileage ceiling and more aggressive recovery weeks.

**Disordered relationship with missed workouts:** Athletes who run injured, run on no sleep, or run sick to avoid falling behind are demonstrating a psychological pattern that will eventually produce injury or burnout. Address directly at intake.

**High anxiety:** Athletes with high baseline anxiety benefit from explicit training-load communication — explaining why easy days are physiologically necessary so the athlete has a rational framework to counter the anxious impulse to do more.

### 5.4 Training History Red Flags

**No base before beginning the plan:**
An athlete running fewer than 25 miles per week for fewer than 8 weeks before starting a plan is under-prepared. Consider adding a 4–6 week base-building phase before beginning the formal plan.

**Catastrophic second half in first marathon:**
A runner who walked miles 18–26 was either under-trained, over-paced, under-fueled, or all three. Detailed investigation of what went wrong is required; pacing strategy, fueling protocol, and long run structure must all be revisited.

## 6. Assessment Output

A completed assessment should produce the following, in writing, shared with the athlete before training begins.

### 6.1 Training Paces

| Pace Zone | Purpose | Effort Description |
|---|---|---|
| Easy / Recovery | 80%+ of weekly volume | Fully conversational, could sustain for hours |
| Long Run | Extended aerobic development | Easy to moderate |
| Marathon Pace (MP) | Race simulation, key workout anchor | Comfortably hard, slightly labored breathing |
| Threshold / Tempo | Lactate threshold development | Hard, sustainable for 20–40 minutes |
| Interval | VO2max development | Very hard, sustainable for 3–5 minutes |

Express paces per mile and per kilometer, as ranges (not single values). Include HR ranges for athletes with reliable HR data.

### 6.2 Weekly Structure

A proposed weekly template that accounts for:
- Number of available days
- Life schedule constraints (which days are hard for training)
- When key workouts fall relative to the long run

**Example template for a 5-day-per-week athlete:**

| Day | Session |
|---|---|
| Monday | Rest or easy cross-training |
| Tuesday | Key workout (tempo or intervals) |
| Wednesday | Easy run, 6–8 miles |
| Thursday | Easy run, 5–7 miles |
| Friday | Rest or very easy 4 miles |
| Saturday | Long run |
| Sunday | Easy recovery run, 4–6 miles |

### 6.3 Target Weekly Mileage Progression

- **Weeks 1–4 (base establishment):** X–Y miles per week
- **Weeks 5–10 (build phase):** Y–Z miles per week, peak week at end of this block
- **Weeks 11–14 (peak):** Hold near peak mileage, race-specific workouts
- **Weeks 15–18 (taper):** Structured mileage reduction, pace maintained

### 6.4 Marathon Goal and Race Scenario

Document:
- **Current fitness VDOT:** The number from which all decisions flow
- **Training VDOT (conservatism-adjusted):** What the plan is actually built around
- **Marathon goal pace range:** A 30-second band (e.g., 9:00–9:30/mile), not a single number
- **A goal:** Realistic given current data and well-executed training
- **B goal:** Conservative — a time that represents success even if training is interrupted
- **Race scenario:** How the athlete should execute the race — first half, miles 18–22, miles 22–finish

### 6.5 Identified Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Recurring left IT band tightness | Physio assessment in week 1; weekly check-in on symptoms; reduce mileage if pain returns |
| High work stress in weeks 8–10 | Pre-approved workout downgrades for those weeks; sleep over workouts |
| Tendency to go out too fast in long runs | GPS pace alerts; explicit first-mile walk or very slow jog protocol |
| History of GI distress during races | Systematic fueling protocol testing beginning in week 4 |
| Ferritin historically low | Blood panel in week 2; supplement if indicated |

### 6.6 Communication Agreement

Document mutual commitments:
- How often coach and athlete will check in (weekly minimum during build phase)
- What data the athlete will report (mileage, perceived effort, sleep, any pain)
- What the trigger conditions are for a mandatory conversation (injury symptoms, two consecutive poor sleep nights, significant life event)
- How goal adjustments will be handled if mid-training data warrants it

The assessment is not a fixed contract. It is a starting point. A midcycle goal revision based on evidence is a sign of good coaching, not failure.

## Appendix: Key Reference Points by Method

| Method | Key Principle for Second Marathoners |
|---|---|
| Daniels' Running Formula | VDOT-derived paces; reassess after 6 weeks; do not assume aspirational fitness |
| Hansons Marathon Method | MP must be realistic from week one; cumulative fatigue is the adaptation mechanism |
| 80/20 Running | 80%+ of volume genuinely easy; if you can't hold this ratio, goal pace is too fast |
| Pfitzinger's Advanced Marathoning | Assumes a strong existing base; requires honest mileage history assessment |
| Sports Psychology Research | Goal rigidity predicts injury and post-race depression; psychological flexibility is a training variable |
