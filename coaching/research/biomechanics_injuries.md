# Running Biomechanics & Injury Prevention
> Coaching reference — recreational & second-marathon focus

---

## 1. Running Biomechanics

### 1.1 The Gait Cycle

The running gait cycle differs fundamentally from walking: there is a **float phase** (both feet airborne) rather than a double-support phase.

| Phase | % of Cycle | Key Events |
|---|---|---|
| Initial contact | ~0% | Foot strikes ground |
| Loading response | 0–15% | Rapid loading, pronation, shock absorption |
| Midstance | 15–40% | Single-leg support, CoM directly over foot |
| Terminal stance / propulsion | 40–60% | Heel rise, hip extension, glute drive |
| Pre-swing | 60–65% | Toe-off, leg leaves ground |
| Initial swing | 65–75% | Hip flexion, knee lifts forward |
| Mid-swing | 75–85% | Leg pendulum, foot clears ground |
| Terminal swing | 85–100% | Leg decelerates, prepares for contact |

**Ground contact time (GCT)** is the single most trainable determinant of running economy. Recreational marathoners average 250–300 ms; elites average 160–200 ms.

**Key coaching insight:** Most recreational runners spend too long in stance and not enough time using elastic energy return from tendons. Cues that help: "Run tall, light feet, quick turnover."

### 1.2 Cadence

#### Optimal Ranges

| Population | Steps per Minute (SPM) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Recreational runners | 160–170 SPM | Typical self-selected; often suboptimal |
| Trained/intermediate | 170–180 SPM | Target zone for most training paces |
| Sub-elite marathon | 180–185 SPM | Diminishing returns above this |
| Elite distance runners | 185–200 SPM | At race pace; not a recreational target |

> **Important caveat:** Cadence scales with pace. A recreational runner doing easy jogging at 5:30/km may comfortably run at 162 SPM; that same runner at tempo effort naturally steps up to 172–176 SPM. Do not chase a single cadence number across all paces.

#### Evidence Base

A 2011 study by Heiderscheit et al. found that a 10% increase in preferred cadence reduced peak hip adduction moment, peak hip flexion angle, and energy absorption at the knee — all factors linked to patellofemoral pain and IT band syndrome. A 5–10% increase is the clinically recommended starting point.

Overstriding (landing with foot far ahead of CoM) is the primary mechanical consequence of low cadence and is associated with increased braking forces and tibial stress.

#### How to Improve Cadence

1. **Measure baseline** — use a GPS watch with cadence, a phone metronome app, or count steps for 30 seconds × 2.
2. **Target a 5% increase first** — if baseline is 160 SPM, target 168 SPM; do not jump straight to 180.
3. **Use a metronome** — apps like Garmin Coach, MetroTimer, or RunMetronome. Use during easy runs only initially.
4. **Cadence-focused strides** — 4–6 × 20-second "quick feet" drills at end of easy runs.
5. **Timeframe** — 6–8 weeks of consistent work to shift self-selected cadence.
6. **Do not force cadence at all paces simultaneously** — start with easy/recovery runs, then extend to moderate efforts.

**Coaching cue:** "Imagine running over hot coals — light, quick, no lingering."

### 1.3 Foot Strike

#### The Three Patterns

| Strike Pattern | Contact Point | Prevalence (recreational marathoners) |
|---|---|---|
| Rearfoot (heel strike) | Posterior heel | ~75–80% |
| Midfoot | Ball of foot + heel simultaneously | ~15–20% |
| Forefoot | Metatarsal heads, heel may not contact | ~5% |

#### Evidence on Injury Rates

The evidence **does not support universally prescribing a forefoot or midfoot strike** for recreational runners:

- **Larson et al. (2011)** — 74% heel strikers at Boston Marathon; foot strike pattern alone did not predict injury history.
- **Daoud et al. (2012)** — forefoot strikers had half the injury rate of heel strikers, but this was in habitually barefoot/minimalist runners — a highly selected population.
- **Kulmala et al. (2013)** — Heel strikers had greater knee loads; forefoot strikers had greater ankle/Achilles loads. Different injury sites, not fewer injuries.

**Clinical bottom line:** Foot strike pattern shifts loading between body segments rather than eliminating load. An abrupt, uncoached strike pattern change in a recreational runner is a significant injury risk. Changes should only be pursued when:
- There is a clear mechanical rationale (e.g., persistent knee overload with heel striking)
- Transition is gradual over 8–12+ weeks
- Calf and Achilles capacity has been specifically built up first

**Coaching cue for overstrikers:** "Land with your foot under your hips, not in front of you."

### 1.4 Posture and Arm Swing

#### Posture

| Element | Optimal Mechanics | Common Error | Cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Head position | Neutral, eyes on horizon ~20m ahead | Looking at feet | "Tall crown, soft chin" |
| Shoulders | Relaxed, level, ~5–10° forward lean from ankles | Shrugging, hunching | "Drop your shoulders from your ears" |
| Torso | Slight forward lean from ankles (not waist) | Sitting in the hips, backward lean | "Lean into your run like you're falling forward slightly" |
| Hips | Extended at toe-off, neutral pelvis | Excessive anterior pelvic tilt, hip drop | "Tall hips, squeeze glutes at push-off" |
| Core | Stiff enough to transfer force, not rigid | Excessive rotation, lateral sway | "Brace lightly, breathe freely" |

**Hip drop (Trendelenburg sign):** Visible contralateral hip drop during midstance is a reliable indicator of gluteus medius weakness. This is one of the most correctable biomechanical risk factors for IT band syndrome, patellofemoral pain, and iliotibial friction.

#### Arm Swing

- **Elbow angle:** 85–100° (roughly a right angle). Hands relaxed, not clenched.
- **Swing direction:** Forward-backward, not crossing the midline of the body. Excessive crossover creates rotational torque that the core and IT band must resist.
- **Range of motion:** Hands swing from approximately hip height to lower chest height during easy running.
- **Asymmetric arm swing** is often a marker of asymmetric lower limb loading — investigate the opposite hip/leg for tightness or injury.

**Coaching cue:** "Imagine you're holding potato chips in each hand — don't crush them." / "Drive elbows back, not across."

## 2. Common Running Injuries

### 2.1 IT Band Syndrome (ITBS)

#### Symptoms
- Sharp or burning pain at the lateral knee, occasionally proximal (lateral hip/greater trochanter)
- Pain begins after a consistent distance into a run ("the 3 km wall")
- Pain with stairs, especially descending
- Tender to palpation at the lateral femoral epicondyle

#### Causes and Risk Factors

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Rapid mileage increase | Most common precipitant |
| Excessive downhill running | Increases knee flexion angle at landing |
| Gluteus medius weakness | Leads to hip adduction, increasing IT band tension |
| Hip abductor/TFL tightness | Increases band tension |
| Cambered roads | Running consistently on the same side |
| Worn shoes | Lateral heel breakdown increases varus moment |

#### Coaching Red Flags
- Pain that starts earlier and earlier in runs (progressive onset shift)
- Pain that persists at rest or overnight
- Lateral knee swelling

---

### 2.2 Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome / MTSS)

#### Symptoms
- Diffuse, achy pain along the medial border of the tibia, typically in the distal two-thirds
- Pain at the start of a run that often warms up and eases, then returns afterward
- Tenderness over a broad area (>5 cm) when pressing along the medial tibia border

**Distinguishing MTSS from stress fracture (critical):**

| Feature | MTSS | Tibial Stress Fracture |
|---|---|---|
| Tenderness | Diffuse (>5 cm) | Focal point tenderness (<2 cm) |
| Pain onset in run | Warms up | Worsens with activity |
| Hop test | Usually negative | Often positive |
| Night pain | Uncommon | More common |

#### Risk Factors

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Female sex | 1.5–3.5× higher risk |
| Low bone density / RED-S | Significant hormonal and nutritional contribution |
| Previous MTSS | Strongest single predictor of recurrence |
| High weekly mileage increase | >10% week-over-week |
| Running on hard surfaces | Concrete > asphalt > track > trail |
| Inadequate calcium/vitamin D | Reduces bone remodeling capacity |

---

### 2.3 Plantar Fasciitis

#### Symptoms
- Sharp, stabbing pain at the heel, typically on the medial plantar surface
- **Worst with first steps in the morning** — the hallmark symptom
- Improves after warming up, worsens again with prolonged loading
- Tight calf / reduced ankle dorsiflexion commonly present

#### Risk Factors

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Reduced ankle dorsiflexion | <10° strongly associated |
| Calf tightness | Directly increases plantar fascia tension |
| Sudden mileage increase | Classic precipitant |
| Body weight | Higher BMI increases plantar loading |
| Transition to minimalist footwear | Removes heel lift, increases fascia stress |

---

### 2.4 Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome / PFPS)

#### Symptoms
- Diffuse, aching pain around or behind the kneecap
- Worsens with prolonged sitting (theater sign / movie sign)
- Worsens with stairs (especially descending), squatting, running downhill
- No significant joint swelling

#### Risk Factors

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Hip abductor/external rotator weakness | Increases dynamic knee valgus → lateral patellar compression |
| Excessive hip adduction | Often from weak glutes; worsens patellofemoral contact pressure |
| Low cadence / overstriding | Increases patellofemoral stress per step |
| Female sex | 2× higher prevalence |
| Running downhills | Significantly increases patellofemoral compressive force |

---

### 2.5 Stress Fractures

Running-related stress fractures most commonly affect:

| Location | Risk Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tibia (midshaft, posteromedial) | Moderate | Most common in runners |
| Metatarsals (2nd and 3rd) | Moderate | Often called "march fractures" |
| Navicular | HIGH | Non-weight-bearing; surgery risk if delayed |
| Femoral neck | HIGH | Non-weight-bearing immediately; surgical risk |
| Sacrum / pelvis | HIGH | Often delayed diagnosis |
| Fibula | Low | Usually manageable conservatively |

#### Symptoms
- Focal, point-tender pain that worsens progressively through a run
- Pain at rest or at night (in higher-grade fractures)
- Positive hop test (single-leg hop reproduces or worsens pain)

#### High-Risk Populations
- Female runners with menstrual irregularity or amenorrhea (RED-S / Female Athlete Triad)
- Low bone mineral density (any age)
- History of prior stress fracture (recurrence risk 40–60%)
- Vegetarian/vegan runners with inadequate calcium/vitamin D

> **Critical note:** Navicular and femoral neck stress fractures must be treated as medical emergencies. Immediate cessation of weight-bearing running, urgent referral for MRI, and orthopedic consultation are mandatory.

## 3. Injury Prevention Strategies

### 3.1 Strength Training for Runners

Strength training reduces running injury risk by approximately **50%** in recreational runners (Lauersen et al., 2014, British Journal of Sports Medicine). It is the single highest-evidence injury prevention intervention available to coaches.

#### Key Muscle Groups and Exercises

| Muscle Group | Running Function | Key Exercises |
|---|---|---|
| Gluteus maximus | Hip extension at push-off, trunk stability | Hip thrust, deadlift, single-leg RDL |
| Gluteus medius | Contralateral hip drop control, frontal plane stability | Side-lying clam, lateral band walk, single-leg squat |
| Quadriceps | Shock absorption on landing, knee control | Bulgarian split squat, step-down, leg press |
| Calf complex (gastrocnemius + soleus) | Energy storage and return, plantarflexion | Single-leg calf raise (straight and bent knee) |
| Core | Force transfer between upper and lower body | Dead bug, Pallof press, single-arm farmer carry |

#### Recommended Programming

**Off-season / base phase (12+ weeks out):**
- 2–3 sessions/week, 30–45 minutes
- Emphasis on building strength: 6–10 reps, 3–4 sets
- Include bilateral and unilateral exercises

**Race build phase (8–12 weeks out):**
- 1–2 sessions/week
- Shift to strength-endurance and plyometrics: 10–15 reps, explosive intent

**Taper (3–4 weeks out):**
- 1 session/week, reduced volume (maintain intensity)

#### Priority Exercises

1. **Single-leg calf raise (bent knee)** — targets soleus; the most underloaded muscle in recreational runners
2. **Single-leg RDL** — combines hamstring strength with gluteus medius recruitment and balance
3. **Copenhagen adductor plank** — hip adductor strength; highly evidence-based
4. **Step-down** — eccentric quadriceps control; directly mimics loading response phase
5. **Dead bug** — contralateral limb coordination, core anti-extension

---

### 3.2 Mobility Work

#### What the Evidence Supports

Isolated static stretching before running **does not prevent injury** and may acutely reduce power output.

| Type | Timing | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Dynamic mobility / activation | Pre-run (5–10 min) | Prepare joints, activate muscles, raise tissue temperature |
| Static stretching | Post-run or separate session | Restore resting length, address specific restrictions |
| Foam rolling / soft tissue | Pre- or post-run | Short-term reduction in perceived tightness |

#### Pre-Run Dynamic Routine (~8 min)

1. Leg swings (forward-back) — 10 each leg
2. Leg swings (side-to-side) — 10 each leg
3. Hip circles — 10 each direction
4. Walking lunges with rotation — 10 each leg
5. Reverse lunge with knee drive — 10 each leg
6. Lateral shuffle — 20 m each direction
7. High knees — 30 m
8. Butt kicks — 30 m
9. A-skip — 30 m
10. Ankle circles + single-leg calf raises — 10 each

#### Key Flexibility Targets

| Restriction | Clinical Significance | Target Stretches |
|---|---|---|
| Ankle dorsiflexion (<10°) | Plantar fasciitis, Achilles overload, patellofemoral stress | Bent-knee wall stretch (soleus), straight-knee wall stretch (gastroc) |
| Hip flexor tightness | Anterior pelvic tilt, reduced hip extension at push-off | Low lunge / couch stretch |
| Hip internal rotation restriction | IT band tension, patellofemoral tracking | 90/90 hip stretch, pigeon |
| Thoracic spine extension stiffness | Forces compensation at lumbar spine | Foam roller thoracic extension |
| Hamstring tightness | Posterior pelvic tilt, altered stride mechanics | Supine hamstring stretch |

### 3.3 Load Management

#### The 10% Rule

**The principle:** Do not increase weekly mileage by more than 10% from one week to the next.

**Evidence base:** The 10% rule is widely cited but has limited direct randomized controlled trial support. It originated from clinical observation, not controlled study. What is better-supported:

1. **Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR)** — The ratio of the current week's load to the average of the past 4 weeks. A ratio of 0.8–1.3 is associated with lower injury risk. Above 1.5 = significantly elevated risk.
2. **Cumulative load matters more than any single week** — Total weekly distance thresholds are independently associated with higher injury risk regardless of progression rate.
3. **Consistency trumps peak mileage** — Runners who train consistently without large swings have consistently lower injury rates.

#### Practical Load Management Principles

| Principle | Specifics |
|---|---|
| 3:1 build cycle | 3 weeks of progressive load → 1 recovery week (~20–30% mileage reduction) |
| Long run rule | Long run should not exceed 30% of total weekly mileage |
| Double stress rule | Do not increase mileage AND intensity in the same week |
| Surface changes | Treat significant surface changes as a load increase |
| Shoe changes | Treat significant footwear changes as a load event; introduce new shoes gradually |
| Life stress | High psychological stress increases injury risk — check in holistically |
| Sleep as a metric | Less than 6 hours/night is independently associated with increased injury risk |

## 4. Return-to-Run Protocols

> General principles before all protocols:
> - Pain must be ≤1/10 during and after all activities before progressing
> - No pain the morning after a session before progressing
> - Strength and flexibility work continues throughout return-to-run
> - Progress one variable at a time (distance OR intensity, not both)

### 4.1 IT Band Syndrome Return-to-Run

**Typical time away:** 2–6 weeks depending on severity

**Phase 1: Acute management (Week 1–2)**
- Complete rest from running
- Continue cycling, swimming, aqua jogging if pain-free
- Begin glute strengthening immediately (clamshells, bridges, lateral band walks)
- Do NOT aggressively foam roll the IT band — it compresses an already irritated structure

**Phase 2: Return to running (Week 2–4)**

| Session | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Session 1 | 10 min easy | Flat surface; stop if pain >1/10 |
| Session 2 (2 days later) | 15 min easy | If session 1 pain-free at 24h |
| Session 3 | 20 min easy | Continue only if pain-free |
| Week 3 | 3 × 20–25 min | Add gentle hills when flat running is consistently pain-free |
| Week 4 | 3 × 25–30 min | Begin reintroducing normal training structure |

**Key biomechanical cues:**
- Increase cadence 5–8%
- Focus on level running surfaces (avoid cambered roads)
- Address hip drop with step-down and single-leg squat quality

---

### 4.2 MTSS Return-to-Run

**Typical time away:** 3–8 weeks (longer if stress reaction confirmed on MRI)

> If a tibial stress fracture has been ruled out and MTSS is confirmed, the following applies.

**Phase 1: Offloading (Week 1–2)**
- Cease running entirely
- Pool running, cycling (if pain-free) permitted

**Phase 2: Return protocol (Week 3 onward)**

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | Walk 20 min, pain-free |
| Day 3 | Walk 30 min, pain-free |
| Day 5 | Walk 40 min |
| Day 7 | Walk/jog: 1 min jog / 2 min walk × 8 |
| Day 9 | Walk/jog: 2 min jog / 1 min walk × 8 |
| Day 11 | Jog 20 min continuous |
| Day 14 | Jog 25 min continuous |
| Day 18 | Jog 30 min |
| Day 21 | Begin structured training |

---

### 4.3 Plantar Fasciitis Return-to-Run

**Typical time away:** 4–12 weeks (highly variable; chronic cases can take 6+ months)

**Phase 1: Symptom management (Week 1–3)**
- Reduce or eliminate running
- Night splint if severe morning pain is present
- Calf stretching program: 3 × 30 seconds, 3–5 times daily (both straight and bent knee)
- **Intrinsic foot strengthening:** short-foot exercise, toe yoga, towel scrunches

**Phase 2: Return to running (Week 3–6)**
- Begin when first-step morning pain is ≤2/10 and walking is pain-free
- Start with 10–15 min easy running on soft surfaces (grass, trail)
- Progress by 10% weekly

**Red flag in PF return:** Pain that progressively worsens through a run (rather than easing after warm-up) may suggest an evolving calcaneal stress fracture — refer for imaging.

---

### 4.4 Patellofemoral Pain Return-to-Run

**Typical time away:** 2–8 weeks

**Phase 2: Return protocol**

| Week | Protocol |
|---|---|
| Week 1 | 15 min flat, easy running. No hills, no stairs. |
| Week 2 | 20–25 min flat. Introduce gentle grades only if pain-free. |
| Week 3 | 25–30 min. Begin incorporating modest elevation if asymptomatic. |
| Week 4+ | Standard 10% progression |

**Key biomechanical cues:**
- Increase cadence 5–10% (most robust intervention; consistently reduces patellofemoral stress)
- Cue "forward trunk lean" to reduce knee extensor moment
- Avoid pronounced downhill running until fully asymptomatic for 4+ weeks

---

### 4.5 Stress Fracture Return-to-Run

> All stress fracture returns should be supervised by a sports medicine physician or orthopedic specialist.

**Low-risk site (fibula, metatarsals 2–4):**
- Typical full return: 8–12 weeks post-diagnosis

**Moderate-risk site (tibia, metatarsals 1/5, calcaneus):**
- Full return: 12–16 weeks
- Mandatory imaging before return to running

**High-risk site (navicular, femoral neck, sacrum, anterior cortex of tibia):**
- Non-weight-bearing mandatory: orthopedic consultation urgently
- Return to sport: 16–24+ weeks, physician-directed only

## 5. Warning Signs: Medical Referral vs Self-Management

### Refer Immediately (Same Day / Emergency)

| Sign | Reason |
|---|---|
| Chest pain, palpitations, or dizziness during running | Cardiac event; call emergency services |
| Sudden severe "pop" with immediate inability to bear weight | Possible fracture or complete ligament rupture |
| Focal tibial or femoral pain with positive hop test | Possible high-risk stress fracture — non-weight-bearing until assessed |
| Heat stroke symptoms (confusion, stopped sweating, temperature >40°C) | Medical emergency |

### Refer Urgently (Within 1–3 Days)

| Sign | Reason |
|---|---|
| Night pain at a bony site | Stress fracture until proven otherwise |
| Painful hip that worsens through a run | Femoral neck stress fracture |
| Midfoot dorsal pain with swelling | Navicular stress fracture |
| Knee with significant swelling after a run | Possible intra-articular pathology |
| Numbness or tingling radiating down a limb | Nerve compression / compartment syndrome |
| Achilles tendon pain with visible swelling and palpable defect | Possible partial or complete rupture |

### Refer Within 1–2 Weeks

| Sign | Reason |
|---|---|
| Injury not improving after 2 weeks of appropriate self-management | Diagnosis may need confirmation |
| Recurrent same-site injury (third episode+) | Structural or nutritional factor needs investigation |
| Menstrual irregularity or amenorrhea in female runners | RED-S / Female Athlete Triad |
| Unexplained weight loss + high training volume | RED-S risk, possible eating disorder |
| Pain that is consistently worsening despite reduced load | Imaging indicated |

### Appropriate for Coached Self-Management

| Condition | Self-Management Criteria |
|---|---|
| IT band syndrome | Lateral knee pain, classic warmup pattern, no swelling; responsive to load reduction + strengthening |
| Mild MTSS | Diffuse medial tibial pain, no focal tenderness, resolves with 1–2 days rest |
| Mild plantar fasciitis | Morning heel pain, pain-free within 10 min of activity, no trauma |
| Patellofemoral pain | Anterior knee pain without swelling, classic theater sign |
| General muscle soreness | Bilateral, diffuse, onset 24–48h post-run, resolves within 72h |

> **Coaching rule of thumb:** If in doubt, refer. A conservative referral that turns out to be unnecessary is far better than delayed diagnosis of a stress fracture or significant joint pathology.

## 6. Recreational vs Elite Injury Risk

### Overall Injury Rates

| Population | Annual Injury Incidence | Most Common Injuries |
|---|---|---|
| Recreational runners (<50 km/week) | 37–56% sustain at least one injury per year | PFPS, ITBS, MTSS, PF |
| Sub-elite / competitive (50–100 km/week) | 45–65% | Achilles tendinopathy, stress fractures, ITBS |
| Elite (>100 km/week) | 40–90% (race season) | Hamstring, stress fracture, Achilles |

### Key Differences in Injury Profile

| Factor | Recreational Runners | Elite Runners |
|---|---|---|
| Primary injury mechanism | Sudden load spikes, poor biomechanics | Cumulative overuse, extreme volume |
| Biomechanical proficiency | Wide variability; rarely coached on mechanics | Highly optimized through years of coaching |
| Strength training compliance | Low — often zero | Standard part of professional program |
| Recovery tools | Often undervalued | Prioritized: sleep, nutrition, massage, physio |
| Most common knee injury | Patellofemoral pain (PFPS) | Patellar tendinopathy (not PFPS) |
| Medical support | Typically none or GP-level | Dedicated sports medicine team |

### The Recreational Runner's Unique Risk Profile

1. **The "now I know what I'm doing" trap** — Second-time marathoners are statistically more likely to overtrain than first-timers. Confidence from the first finish leads to aggressive training the body is not prepared for.

2. **Competing life demands** — Sleep debt, psychological stress, and skipped recovery are common and meaningfully increase injury risk.

3. **Inadequate strength training** — The majority of recreational runners do zero supplementary strength work. The 50% injury risk reduction associated with strength training represents a massive untapped intervention.

4. **Biomechanical inefficiency persists for years** — Without specific feedback, overstriding, hip drop, and arm crossover can persist throughout a running career.

5. **Shoe and surface transitions** — Recreational runners are more likely to change footwear based on trends without understanding the loading implications.

### Coaching Priorities Specific to Recreational Marathon Runners

| Priority | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Load management education | The 10% rule as a heuristic; ACWR concept simplified |
| Sleep and recovery hygiene | The most underutilized performance and injury prevention tool |
| Two strength sessions minimum per week | Even body-weight only dramatically reduces risk |
| Red flag education | Athletes must know when to stop and seek help |
| Gait feedback | Even basic cadence monitoring enables simple interventions |
| Nutrition fundamentals | Adequate energy intake, protein (1.6–2.0 g/kg/day), calcium, iron, vitamin D |

## Quick Reference: Injury Comparison Summary

| Injury | Location | Cardinal Sign | Key Cause | First Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IT Band Syndrome | Lateral knee | Pain at consistent run distance | Glute weakness, rapid mileage increase | Reduce load, begin glute strengthening |
| MTSS | Medial tibia (diffuse) | Warms up during run, aches after | Load spike, pronation, female sex | Reduce mileage 50%; rule out stress fracture |
| Plantar Fasciitis | Plantar heel | First-step morning pain | Calf tightness, load increase | Calf stretching, foot strengthening, mileage reduction |
| PFPS | Anterior/peripatellar knee | Theater sign, stairs | Hip weakness, overstriding | Cadence increase, glute and quad strengthening |
| Stress Fracture | Variable | Point tenderness, worsens during run | Rapid load increase, low bone density | Stop running; medical referral for imaging |
