# Exercise Physiology for Marathon Runners
> Coaching reference — recreational & second-marathon focus

---

## 1. Aerobic vs Anaerobic Energy Systems

### Overview

The human body produces ATP (adenosine triphosphate — the universal cellular fuel) through two primary pathways: aerobic and anaerobic. Running uses both simultaneously, but the dominant system shifts with intensity.

### Aerobic System

- **Fuel**: Fat (triglycerides) and carbohydrates (glucose/glycogen), in the presence of oxygen
- **Byproduct**: CO₂ and water — both easily cleared
- **Efficiency**: Fat yields ~9 kcal/g; carbohydrates yield ~4 kcal/g
- **Fat stores**: A 70 kg runner with 15% body fat carries ~9,000 kcal of fat — theoretically enough for 3–4 marathons
- **Glycogen stores**: ~1,800–2,000 kcal in muscle + liver — enough for roughly 90–120 minutes of moderate-intensity running
- **Speed**: Slower to ramp up, but essentially unlimited in duration at low-to-moderate intensities
- **Dominant at**: Easy running, long runs, anything below ~75–80% HRmax

### Anaerobic System

Two sub-systems:

| Sub-system | Fuel | Duration | Byproduct |
|------------|------|----------|-----------|
| **ATP-PCr** (phosphocreatine) | Creatine phosphate | 0–10 seconds | None |
| **Glycolysis** (anaerobic) | Glucose/glycogen | 10 seconds – ~2 minutes | Lactate + H⁺ ions |

- Anaerobic glycolysis does not require oxygen but produces lactate rapidly
- At very high intensities, lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared — causing the burning sensation and eventual slowdown
- **Key point**: Lactate itself is not the villain — it is a fuel source. The acidosis (H⁺ accumulation) impairs muscle contraction

### Marathon Relevance

Marathon running is overwhelmingly aerobic — roughly **97–99% aerobic** at typical recreational paces. However:
- Even at easy effort, some anaerobic contribution exists
- The efficiency of the aerobic system (how well you burn fat, how economically you use oxygen) is the primary determinant of marathon performance
- "Hitting the wall" at mile 18–20 is largely a carbohydrate depletion event — the aerobic system must shift heavily to fat when glycogen runs out, and fat oxidation alone cannot sustain marathon pace

### Practical Rule of Thumb

> If you can hold a full conversation while running, you are predominantly aerobic. If you are reduced to single words, anaerobic contribution has risen significantly.

## 2. Lactate Threshold

### What It Is

Lactate threshold (LT) describes the exercise intensity above which lactate begins to accumulate in the blood faster than it can be cleared. Two commonly referenced points exist:

| Term | Also Called | Blood Lactate Level | % of HRmax (trained) | % of HRmax (recreational) |
|------|-------------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **LT1** (first threshold) | Aerobic threshold | ~2 mmol/L | ~75–80% | ~65–72% |
| **LT2** (second threshold) | Anaerobic threshold, OBLA | ~4 mmol/L | ~85–92% | ~78–85% |

- **LT1** = the point where lactate first begins rising above baseline. Below LT1, nearly all fuel is cleared aerobically. This is "all-day" pace.
- **LT2** = the highest intensity at which lactate production and clearance are still roughly balanced. Sustainable for 30–60 minutes in trained runners.
- **Marathon race pace** for recreational runners typically sits just below or at LT1, not LT2

### Why It Matters for Marathon Runners

LT is arguably **the single most important physiological variable** for marathon performance in recreational runners — more practically actionable than VO2max.

- A runner with a high LT2 can sustain a faster pace before lactate spirals out of control
- LT2 pace correlates strongly with marathon performance in the 3:30–5:30 range
- Training LT1 upward means you burn more fat and less glycogen at a given pace — directly combating the wall
- Research suggests recreational runners can improve LT by **10–20%** over a structured training cycle

### Field Estimation of Lactate Threshold (No Lab Required)

**LT2 pace estimation methods:**
1. **30-minute time trial**: Warm up 10 min, run 30 min at maximum sustainable effort; average pace ≈ LT2 pace (±5–10 seconds/mile)
2. **Conversation test**: LT2 ≈ the pace where you can speak only 3–4 words before needing a breath
3. **HR estimation**: LT2 HR ≈ 85–90% of HRmax for most recreational runners
4. **RPE**: LT2 ≈ 7/10 perceived effort — "comfortably hard"

**LT1 pace** is typically 45–75 seconds/mile slower than LT2 pace for recreational runners.

### How to Train the Lactate Threshold

#### Tempo Runs (LT2-focused)
- **Pace**: LT2 pace ± 10 sec/mile
- **Duration**: 20–40 minutes continuous; beginners start at 2×10 min with 2-min recovery
- **Frequency**: Once per week during build phase

#### Cruise Intervals (LT2-focused)
- **Format**: 3–5 × 8–15 min at LT2 pace with 1–2 min easy jog recovery
- **Advantage**: Higher total volume at threshold than a single tempo run

#### Long Easy Runs (LT1-focused)
- Running consistently at or slightly below LT1 (easy/conversational pace) over months develops fat oxidation capacity, raises LT1 absolutely, and improves economy
- **The most underrated LT1 tool for recreational runners**: Simply run more easy miles

#### Summary Table

| Workout Type | Intensity | Duration per session | Training target |
|-------------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Long easy run | 65–72% HRmax | 90 min–3+ hrs | LT1, fat oxidation |
| Steady-state | 75–80% HRmax | 40–60 min | LT1/LT2 bridge |
| Tempo run | 83–88% HRmax | 20–40 min | LT2 |
| Cruise intervals | 83–88% HRmax | 3–5 × 8–15 min | LT2 volume |

## 3. VO2max

### What It Is

VO2max is the maximum rate at which the body can consume oxygen during exhaustive exercise. It represents the ceiling of the aerobic engine.

- **Units**: mL of O₂ per kg of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min)
- **Average sedentary adult**: 30–40 mL/kg/min
- **Recreational trained runner**: 45–60 mL/kg/min
- **Elite male marathoner**: 70–85 mL/kg/min (e.g., Kipchoge ~92 mL/kg/min)

### How It Relates to Marathon Performance

VO2max sets the ceiling, but the **percentage of VO2max you can sustain** at marathon pace determines performance. This percentage is directly tied to lactate threshold.

**Marathon pace approximations by VO2max** (Daniels formula, male, sea level):

| VO2max (mL/kg/min) | Estimated marathon time |
|--------------------|-------------------------|
| 35 | ~5:30 |
| 40 | ~4:45 |
| 45 | ~4:10 |
| 50 | ~3:40 |
| 55 | ~3:17 |
| 60 | ~2:58 |

### The Three Determinants of Marathon Performance

1. **VO2max** — the size of the aerobic engine
2. **Lactate threshold** (% of VO2max at LT2) — how hard you can push the engine continuously
3. **Running economy** — how efficiently the engine converts oxygen into forward motion

For recreational runners, **all three are highly trainable** — and economy and LT improvements often produce more near-term marathon gains than VO2max itself.

### How Trainable Is VO2max for Non-Athletes?

**Short answer: Very trainable early, less so later.**

- **Untrained to recreational**: VO2max improvements of **15–25%** are common in the first 1–2 years of consistent training
- **Recreational to competitive**: Improvements slow to **5–10%** over additional years
- **Genetic ceiling**: Roughly 50% of VO2max is genetically determined
- **Response rate**: Most adaptations occur within **8–12 weeks** of starting a new VO2max-targeted stimulus

### What Drives VO2max Improvements

1. Increased stroke volume (more blood pumped per heartbeat) → higher cardiac output
2. Capillarization of muscles → better O₂ delivery
3. Mitochondrial density → better O₂ utilization at the cellular level
4. Blood volume expansion → more oxygen-carrying capacity

### How to Train VO2max

VO2max is best trained at **vVO2max** — the minimum speed that elicits VO2max, typically sustained for 6–8 minutes in trained runners.

#### VO2max Intervals
- **Pace**: ~3K–5K race pace (roughly 95–100% HRmax)
- **Format**: 4–6 × 3–5 min with equal or slightly shorter recovery (e.g., 5 × 4 min on / 3 min off)
- **Total work**: 16–24 min per session at VO2max intensity
- **Frequency**: Once per week maximum; every 10–14 days for many recreational runners
- **Recovery**: Full recovery (48–72 hours) before and after

#### Field Estimation of VO2max
- **Cooper test**: Run as far as possible in 12 minutes; VO2max ≈ (distance in meters − 504.9) / 44.73
- **Garmin/Wearable estimate**: Derived from HR + pace data; ±10% accuracy vs lab, useful for tracking trends
- **Race result**: Online calculators (Daniels, McMillan) back-calculate VO2max from race times

### VO2max vs LT: Where to Focus Training Time

| Priority | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **Easy aerobic volume (LT1)** — 80% of training | Builds the base, raises LT1, improves fat burning |
| **Threshold work (LT2)** — 15% of training | Direct marathon-pace adaptation |
| **VO2max intervals** — 5% of training | Useful in build phase; raises ceiling; high injury risk if overdone |

## 4. Heart Rate Training Zones

### Why Heart Rate Zones Matter

Heart rate is a proxy for exercise intensity — it reflects how hard the cardiovascular system is working. Training to HR rather than pace accounts for heat, fatigue, hills, and individual variation.

**Limitations of HR-based training:**
- **Cardiac drift**: HR rises over a long run even at constant pace
- **Day-to-day variability**: Stress, sleep, caffeine, dehydration all shift HR ±5–10 bpm
- **Heat**: HR runs ~5–10 bpm higher in hot weather at identical effort
- **HR lag**: Takes 60–90 seconds to respond to intensity changes
- **Individual variation**: Two runners with identical HRmax can have dramatically different LTs

### Step 1: Determine HRmax

**Do not use 220 − age.** Error margin is ±10–15 bpm — too large to be useful.

Better methods:
1. **Field test**: After thorough warm-up, run 2 × 800m at maximal effort with minimal rest; highest observed HR ≈ HRmax
2. **Race data**: Highest HR recorded in the final 400m of a hard 5K effort
3. **Wearable estimate**: Reasonably accurate for trend tracking

### Step 2: Determine Lactate Threshold HR (LTHR)

- Perform the 30-minute time trial described in section 2
- Average HR over the final **20 minutes** ≈ LTHR (LT2 HR)
- Typical values: 85–90% HRmax for recreational runners

### The 5-Zone Model (Coggan/Friel)

| Zone | Name | % HRmax | % LTHR | RPE | Physiological target |
|------|------|---------|--------|-----|----------------------|
| Z1 | Recovery | <68% | <81% | 1–2 | Active recovery; no meaningful training load |
| Z2 | Aerobic | 68–75% | 81–89% | 2–3 | Fat oxidation; LT1 development; base building |
| Z3 | Tempo/Steady | 76–82% | 90–93% | 4–5 | LT1/LT2 bridge; "comfortably uncomfortable" |
| Z4 | Threshold | 83–90% | 94–99% | 6–7 | LT2; race-specific for half marathon |
| Z5 | VO2max | >90% | >100%* | 8–10 | VO2max; 5K effort and above |

**Example for a runner with HRmax = 185 bpm, LTHR = 162 bpm:**

| Zone | HR Range |
|------|----------|
| Z1 | < 126 bpm |
| Z2 | 126–139 bpm |
| Z3 | 140–151 bpm |
| Z4 | 152–162 bpm |
| Z5 | > 162 bpm |

### The 3-Zone Model (Seiler — polarized training basis)

| Zone | Name | Boundary | Roughly corresponds to |
|------|------|----------|------------------------|
| Zone 1 | Low intensity | Below LT1 | 5-zone Z1+Z2 |
| Zone 2 | Moderate | Between LT1 and LT2 | 5-zone Z3 |
| Zone 3 | High intensity | Above LT2 | 5-zone Z4+Z5 |

**Polarized training distribution (Seiler):** 80% in Zone 1, ~0–5% in Zone 2, 15–20% in Zone 3.

**Why avoid the middle?** Zone 2 (3-zone model) — the "moderate" grind — is too easy to drive VO2max adaptations and too hard to allow full recovery for high-quality Z3 work. Many recreational runners default to this zone on nearly every run ("junk miles" at moderate effort).

### Calculating Individual Zones: Step-by-Step

1. **Find HRmax** — field test or verified wearable data
2. **Find LTHR** — 30-minute time trial, average of final 20 minutes
3. **Apply LTHR-based zones** (more accurate than % HRmax alone):
   - Z2 lower bound: LTHR × 0.81
   - Z2 upper bound: LTHR × 0.89
   - Z4 lower bound: LTHR × 0.94
   - Z4 upper bound: LTHR × 0.99 (≈ LTHR)
   - Z5: above LTHR

### Pros and Cons of HR-Based Training

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Accounts for individual variation | Lag in response to intensity changes |
| Adjusts for heat, fatigue, altitude | Cardiac drift on long runs inflates apparent effort |
| Accessible (every runner has a heart) | Requires accurate HRmax; 220-age is unreliable |
| Good for easy day enforcement | Optical HR sensors can misread during tempo efforts |
| Correlates well with perceived effort when conditions are stable | Emotional stress, caffeine, dehydration distort readings |

### Marathon Pace and Heart Rate

For most recreational runners, **marathon race pace corresponds to Zone 2 (5-zone model)** — approximately 70–78% HRmax, or 81–88% LTHR. If long training runs drift into Zone 3, pace is too fast.

## 5. Periodization for Amateur Marathon Runners

### What Is Periodization?

Periodization is the systematic structuring of training into phases, each with a specific physiological purpose, to peak performance at race day.

### Typical 20-Week Marathon Training Plan Structure

| Phase | Duration | Primary Goal | Weekly Mileage (recreational) |
|-------|----------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| **Base** | Weeks 1–6 | Build aerobic foundation; establish consistency | 25–40 miles (40–65 km) |
| **Build** | Weeks 7–14 | Introduce quality work; increase volume; practice race pace | 35–55 miles (55–90 km) |
| **Peak** | Weeks 15–17 | Maximum training load; simulate race conditions | 45–60 miles (70–95 km) |
| **Taper** | Weeks 18–20 | Allow supercompensation; arrive fresh | Reduce to 40% → 60% → 80% of peak volume |

### Phase 1: Base Phase (Weeks 1–6)

**Purpose:** Build the aerobic engine that all later work depends on.

**Key characteristics:**
- **80–90% of running at easy effort (Z1–Z2)** — below LT1, fully conversational
- Long run builds from current longest run +10–20% per week; observe the **10% rule**
- No formal speedwork; strides (8–10 × 20-second relaxed accelerations) are acceptable
- **Strength and mobility work** is especially productive now
- Weekly long run: Target 14–16 miles by end of base for 4:00–5:00 marathon target

**Common mistake:** Running base miles too fast. Easy pace should feel embarrassingly slow. In hot weather, HR zones trump pace — slow down to stay in Z2.

**Adaptations targeted:**
- Cardiac stroke volume increases
- Capillary density in Type I muscle fibers
- Mitochondrial biogenesis
- Fat oxidation upregulation
- Tendon and connective tissue conditioning (lags muscular adaptation by 4–6 weeks — rushing this phase causes injury)

### Phase 2: Build Phase (Weeks 7–14)

**Purpose:** Convert aerobic base into marathon-specific fitness.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Volume continues to increase** — typically the highest-mileage weeks of the cycle land here or in peak phase
- **Quality introduced: 1–2 sessions per week** of structured work:
  - Tempo runs or cruise intervals (LT2)
  - Marathon-pace runs (MP runs) — portions of the long run at goal marathon pace
  - Occasional VO2max intervals in later build weeks
- **80/20 rule maintained**: Despite adding quality, 80% of miles remain easy
- Long run reaches 18–20 miles; 2–3 long runs of 18+ miles are standard
- **Midweek medium-long run** (10–14 miles) provides additional aerobic stimulus

**Session structure example (Week 10, 45 miles):**

| Day | Session | Miles |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Mon | Rest or 4-mile easy | 0–4 |
| Tue | 8-mile easy with 4 × 1-mile at MP | 8 |
| Wed | 5-mile easy | 5 |
| Thu | 10-mile with 5-mile tempo at LT2 | 10 |
| Fri | Rest or 4-mile easy | 0–4 |
| Sat | 4–5 mile easy | 5 |
| Sun | 20-mile long run | 20 |

### Phase 3: Peak Phase (Weeks 15–17)

**Purpose:** Maximum training load — highest fitness point of the cycle.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Highest volume weeks** — often 10–20% above average build volume
- Long run: 20–22 miles maximum (21 is a common sweet spot)
- **One "simulation" long run**: 20 miles with miles 12–18 at or near race pace
- The runner should feel moderately fatigued during peak — this is normal

**Injury risk is highest in peak phase.** Monitor for:
- Sharp or localized bone pain (stress fracture risk elevated)
- HR elevated >5–7 bpm above normal at easy paces
- Persistent muscle soreness lasting >72 hours

### Phase 4: Taper (Weeks 18–20)

**Purpose:** Allow supercompensation — the body rebuilds from accumulated training stress while shedding fatigue.

**Taper structure:**

| Week | Volume relative to peak | Quality work | Long run |
|------|------------------------|--------------|----------|
| Week 18 (3 weeks out) | 70–75% | 1 session | 14–16 miles |
| Week 19 (2 weeks out) | 55–60% | 1 short session | 10–12 miles |
| Week 20 / race week | 30–40% | 2–3 short race-pace efforts | 6–8 miles Tuesday, minimal thereafter |

**Taper physiology:**
- Muscle glycogen levels rise ~20% above normal after 3-week taper
- Blood volume expands slightly
- Muscle repair completes
- Neuromuscular efficiency peaks

**Taper madness is real.** Recreational runners almost universally feel terrible during taper — heavy legs, phantom pains, loss of confidence. Trust the process.

**Intensity during taper:** Do not drop intensity — drop volume. Including 2–3 short (60–90 second) race-pace or slightly faster efforts on Tuesday–Thursday of race week maintains neuromuscular priming.

## 6. Recreational Runners vs Competitive Athletes — Key Differences

### Physiological Profile

| Variable | Recreational (4:00–5:30 marathon) | Competitive amateur (2:50–3:30 marathon) | Elite |
|----------|-----------------------------------|------------------------------------------|-------|
| VO2max | 40–52 mL/kg/min | 52–65 mL/kg/min | 70–92 mL/kg/min |
| LT2 (% VO2max) | 65–78% | 78–88% | 88–92% |
| Weekly mileage | 25–50 miles | 50–80 miles | 100–140 miles |
| Running economy | Less efficient | More efficient | Highly optimized |
| % at marathon pace from VO2max | 65–75% | 75–82% | 85–92% |

### How These Concepts Apply Differently

#### Aerobic Base Building

- **Recreational**: Far larger gains available. A runner going from 20 to 40 miles/week will see dramatic fitness improvements. Volume is often the single most important lever.
- **Competitive**: Already running significant volume; gains require optimization of quality, not just more miles.
- **Implication**: Recreational runners should not rush to complex workouts — more easy miles often beats more interval sessions.

#### The "80/20 Rule" Compliance

Research by Seiler shows elite athletes naturally gravitate to ~80% low intensity / 20% high intensity. Recreational runners tend toward 50–60% moderate intensity — the "black hole" zone that is too hard for recovery, too easy for adaptation.

**The fix for recreational runners:**
- Make easy days genuinely easy (enforce with HR monitor)
- Make hard days genuinely hard (execute quality sessions fully, not conservatively)
- Eliminate "medium" — the most common and most costly training error

#### Injury Risk and Recovery

- **Recreational runners** have significantly higher injury rates (~65–75% of marathon runners experience a training injury in any given training cycle). Reasons:
  - Less conditioned connective tissue
  - Higher relative training loads vs. current fitness
  - Less experience recognizing warning signs
  - Less time for recovery habits

- **Key implication for program design**: Build recovery weeks (reduce volume by 20–30%) every 3rd or 4th week regardless of how good the runner feels. Connective tissue adaptation lags muscular adaptation by 4–6 weeks.

#### Fueling During Training

- **Recreational**: At paces slower than ~5:30/km (8:50/mile), fat oxidation contributes substantially. Many recreational runners don't need gels for runs under 90 minutes. But glycogen still matters at marathon pace — practice fueling strategy in training.
- **Recreational guideline**: 30–60g carbohydrate/hour for runs over 75–90 minutes. Practice with race-day products in training; GI distress is the most common race-day catastrophe.
