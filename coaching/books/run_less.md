# Run Less, Run Faster (FIRST) — Coaching Synthesis
> Key principles for recreational & second-marathon runners

---

## 1. The 3-Plus-2 Approach

The FIRST program is built on a deceptively simple premise: **quality over quantity**. Instead of running 5–6 days per week at mixed intensities, runners do exactly **three purposeful runs** and **two cross-training sessions** per week — every run has a specific physiological target, and none is "junk mileage."

**Why it works:**
- Each run stresses a distinct energy system or physiological adaptation
- Mandatory rest between hard runs reduces cumulative fatigue and injury risk
- Cross-training maintains aerobic capacity without the impact stress of running
- The structure enforces discipline: there is no "easy day" that accidentally becomes moderate

**Weekly template:**

| Day | Session |
|-----|---------|
| Tuesday | Interval Run (track or measured course) |
| Thursday | Tempo Run |
| Saturday or Sunday | Long Run |
| Wednesday | Cross-Training #1 |
| Friday | Cross-Training #2 |
| Monday / Sunday | Rest |

At least one full rest or cross-training day must separate each key run.

---

## 2. The Three Key Runs

### 2a. Interval Run

**Purpose:** Develop VO2max and running economy. Trains the body to clear lactate efficiently and improves stride mechanics at race effort.

**Intensity:** 5K race pace — the pace at which you could race hard for roughly 20–22 minutes.

**Typical formats:**
- 6 × 800 m at 5K race pace
- 5 × 1000 m at 5K race pace
- 4 × 1200 m or 3 × 1600 m at slightly slower than 5K pace
- Recovery jog: 50–90% of the interval duration

**Key coaching point:** The goal is consistency across all repeats. If the final repeat is dramatically slower than the first, the pace was too aggressive. Start conservatively.

---

### 2b. Tempo Run

**Purpose:** Raise the lactate threshold — the pace you can sustain for roughly one hour of racing. Directly translates to marathon and half-marathon performance.

**Intensity:** Approximately 10K race pace, or roughly 25–30 seconds per mile **slower** than 5K race pace. Breathing is labored but controlled; you can speak in short phrases, not full sentences. RPE: 7–8 out of 10.

**Alternative rule of thumb:** 15–20 seconds per mile faster than goal marathon pace.

**Typical formats:**
- 3–5 miles at tempo pace (base phase)
- Up to 8–10 miles at tempo pace (peak marathon training)
- Cruise intervals: 2 × 3 miles at tempo with 3 min recovery

**Key coaching point:** Many runners run their tempo runs too slowly, converting them into glorified easy runs. The pace must be genuinely challenging.

---

### 2c. Long Run

**Purpose:** Build aerobic endurance, fat metabolism, glycogen storage capacity, and mental resilience. Conditions tendons, ligaments, and slow-twitch muscle fibers.

**Intensity:** Conversational. Full sentences. This is the one run in the FIRST program that prioritizes time on feet over pace.

**Pace:** **45–90 seconds per mile slower than goal marathon pace** (about 60–75 seconds slower than current marathon race pace)

**Typical formats:**
- Peaks at 20–22 miles (approximately 3–4 weeks before race day)
- Weekly increases of roughly 1–2 miles, with a step-back week every 3–4 weeks

**Key coaching point:** Resist the urge to run this faster. The long run is not a tempo run. Running it too fast increases injury risk and compromises recovery for Tuesday's intervals.

## 3. Calculating Paces from a Recent Race Time

**Step 1 — Establish a reference time.** Use the most recent race over 5K, 10K, or half marathon (run within the last 8–12 weeks at genuine race effort).

**Step 2 — Derive 5K equivalent pace.** Use McMillan Running Calculator, Jack Daniels' VDOT tables, or the FIRST tables.

**Step 3 — Apply the pace formulas:**

| Run Type | Pace Relative to 5K Race Pace |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Interval pace | 5K race pace |
| Tempo pace | 5K race pace + 25–30 sec/mile |
| Long run pace | Goal marathon pace + 45–90 sec/mile |

**Worked example — runner with 5K time of 25:00 (8:03/mile):**

| Run | Pace |
|-----|------|
| Interval (800 m repeats) | 8:03/mile → ~4:01 per 800 m |
| Tempo run | ~8:30/mile |
| Long run (targeting 4:15 marathon = 9:44/mile) | ~10:30–11:00/mile |

**Critical principle:** Paces must be **earned** by current fitness, not aspirational goal fitness. Running at a pace based on a target race time you have not yet achieved leads directly to injury.

## 4. Cross-Training Recommendations

Cross-training in FIRST carries a genuine aerobic training load — it is not optional recovery filler.

### Recommended Activities

| Activity | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Cycling** | Most commonly recommended; low impact, highly controllable intensity |
| **Swimming** | Full-body aerobic conditioning with zero impact |
| **Elliptical trainer** | Mimics running motion without impact |
| **Rowing** | Develops posterior chain; high cardiovascular demand |
| **Aqua jogging** | Closely mirrors running biomechanics; requires genuine effort |

**Less recommended:** Yoga or light stretching alone do not fulfill the cross-training requirement.

### Intensity and Duration

- **Duration:** 45–60 minutes per session (can extend to 75 minutes)
- **Intensity:** Moderate to moderately hard — roughly Zone 2–3 heart rate (65–80% of max HR). Should feel like a workout, not a recovery stroll.

**Key coaching point:** Runners who treat cross-training as easy recovery undermine the program. The aerobic adaptation is real, but only if the effort is genuine.

## 5. Who This Plan Works Best For (and Who It Does Not Suit)

### Best fit: runners who...
- Have **limited weekly time** — professionals, parents, anyone who cannot run 6 days per week
- Have a history of **overuse injuries** tied to high mileage
- Are **experienced enough to run at prescribed intensities** — novices often lack the discipline to self-regulate effort accurately
- Already have a **base of consistent running** (at least 6–12 months, able to complete a 10K comfortably)
- Enjoy or tolerate **cross-training activities** — cyclists and triathletes adapt particularly well
- Are running their **second or subsequent marathon** and want a smarter, more structured approach

### Less ideal fit: runners who...
- Are **true beginners** — the interval and tempo intensities require a foundation that brand-new runners do not yet have
- **Love running** as their primary daily activity and find cross-training unrewarding
- Respond best to **high mileage stimulus**
- Are training for **ultra-marathon distances**, where time-on-feet volume is non-negotiable
- Cannot reliably access **cross-training equipment or facilities**

## 6. Applying FIRST to a Recreational Runner Doing Their Second Marathon

A recreational runner preparing for their second marathon is perhaps the ideal FIRST candidate. They have a finishing time from their first marathon (a critical data point for pace-setting), they know what race day demands, and they are motivated to improve.

### Phase 1 — Establish Current Fitness (Weeks 1–4)

Run a recent 5K or 10K at genuine race effort. Do not use the first-marathon finishing time as the pace anchor — it may reflect poor pacing or inadequate training rather than current aerobic fitness.

### Phase 2 — 16-Week Marathon Plan Structure

**Weeks 1–4 (Base building):**
- Long runs: 14–16 miles
- Intervals: 6 × 800 m or 5 × 1000 m
- Tempo: 3–4 miles

**Weeks 5–10 (Build phase):**
- Long runs progress to 18–20 miles
- Interval distances increase (1200 m, 1600 m repeats)
- Tempo runs extend to 6–8 miles

**Weeks 11–14 (Peak):**
- Long run peaks at 20 miles (one time)
- Maximum tempo volume: 8–10 miles
- Interval intensity maintained but volume held steady

**Weeks 15–16 (Taper):**
- All three key runs continue but at reduced volume
- Intensity is maintained — do not slow down during taper
- Race week: final long run of 10–12 miles two weeks out; race week key runs are short and sharp

### Practical coaching notes:
1. **Resist "banking time" in the early miles.** FIRST training at correct paces builds the fitness to run even splits.
2. **The long run pace matters.** Running 90 seconds per mile slower than goal pace feels embarrassing; it is correct.
3. **Track your cross-training effort.** Use heart rate or perceived exertion. Do not allow cross-training days to become passive recovery.
4. **Be precise with intervals.** Use a GPS watch or measured track.
5. **Reassess paces mid-plan if fitness improves.** If you run a tune-up race, update your training paces.

## 7. Comparison to Higher-Mileage Approaches

| Dimension | FIRST (3+2) | High-Mileage Approach |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Weekly runs | 3 | 5–7 |
| Weekly mileage | 25–40 miles | 45–80+ miles |
| Cross-training | Structured, essential | Optional or absent |
| Injury risk | Lower (for injury-prone runners) | Higher with poor recovery |
| Time commitment | Lower | Higher |
| Ceiling for performance | Moderate-high | High |
| Best for | Time-limited, injury-prone, experienced | High-capacity, high-commitment runners |

### What FIRST gives up:
- Raw aerobic volume and its corresponding mitochondrial adaptations
- The "feel" for comfortable marathon pace developed through frequent running

### What FIRST gains:
- Dramatically reduced injury rates in recreational populations
- Higher average quality of each running session
- Sustainable year-round training
- Cross-training benefits with zero impact

**The honest bottom line:** A runner of identical talent on Pfitzinger with perfect execution will likely run faster than on FIRST. But most recreational runners do not execute high-mileage plans consistently. For the runner who trains 3 days/week consistently vs. one who logs 55 miles/week but gets injured every cycle — FIRST wins.

## 8. Key Insights and Coaching Takeaways

1. **Every mile must have a purpose.** Volume without specificity does not produce proportional gains; it produces fatigue and injury.

2. **Pace discipline is the hardest skill.** Most runners run hard days too easy and easy days too hard. FIRST enforces polarization.

3. **Fitness, not aspiration, sets the pace.** Training paces derived from a race two weeks ago are honest. Training paces derived from a goal marathon time are dangerous.

4. **Cross-training is not a consolation prize.** Athletes who approach cross-training with real effort often find their aerobic fitness surpasses what they achieved on run-only plans.

5. **Consistency beats peak weeks.** A runner who completes 15 of 16 weeks in full is better prepared than one who completes 10 brilliantly and misses the rest through injury.

6. **The long run is not a performance test.** Done correctly, it feels uncomfortably slow. Discomfort with slowness is a sign you are doing it right.

7. **Taper anxiety is a feature, not a bug.** Heavy legs during the two-week taper indicate glycogen storage is occurring. Resist the urge to add extra miles.

8. **The program rewards athletes who value health over ego.** FIRST requires the intellectual humility to run slow when the plan says slow.

9. **The 5K is the engine of the whole plan.** All paces derive from the 5K. Running a 5K race periodically during training is not a distraction; it is calibration data.

10. **Second-marathon runners have a structural advantage.** They have race-day experience. When they apply FIRST's structure with honest pacing and disciplined cross-training, meaningful personal records are typically achievable — often 15–30 minutes of improvement.
